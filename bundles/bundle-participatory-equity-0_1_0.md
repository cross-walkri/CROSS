# CROSS+WALKRI Program Bundle: Participatory and Equity-Focused Grantmaking

---

## Overview

This bundle covers grant and funding programs in which community members serve as decision-makers rather than only as beneficiaries, and programs explicitly designed to reduce racial inequity, build power in marginalized communities, or advance self-determination for indigenous and community-led organizations. The ecosystem includes participatory grantmaking programs (community members allocate funds), participatory budgeting (residents allocate public funds through structured democratic processes), racial equity and power-building grantmaking (programs applying NCRP criteria, trust-based philanthropy principles, or equitable evaluation frameworks), and indigenous and community-led development (programs requiring FPIC compliance and centering community self-determination as an outcome).

Four program sub-types operate in this space with materially different obligation structures. This bundle addresses all four and notes where provisions diverge.

**Sub-type A: Participatory grantmaking** places community members in decision-making roles as reviewers, panel members, or allocators. Community members are not merely consulted; they make or substantially determine grant allocation decisions. The program's entry specification gate must specify how community voice is weighted in those decisions, not only what criteria are applied.

**Sub-type B: Participatory budgeting** structures the allocation of public or institutional funds through a democratic process in which affected residents or community members propose, deliberate over, and vote on how funds are spent. The PB Project's 15 Key Metrics define both the process metrics (who participates) and the outcome metrics (what gets funded) that must be pre-specified.

**Sub-type C: Racial equity and power-building grantmaking** applies explicit racial equity criteria to grant allocation and program design, following frameworks such as the National Committee for Responsive Philanthropy (NCRP) racial equity criteria, the Equitable Evaluation Initiative's principles, and trust-based philanthropy practice in equity contexts. Disaggregation by race, income, and geography is a core criterion at the statutory level of CROSS's disaggregation ratchet.

**Sub-type D: Indigenous and community-led development** funds programs in which indigenous communities or other self-defined communities are the primary decision-makers over program design, implementation, and evaluation. Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC) is a pre-condition for the entry specification gate: community consent to the program design must be documented before any application opens.

## Part 1: CROSS Runbook

### Obligation Mode Selection

**Sub-types A and B (participatory grantmaking, participatory budgeting):** Use Change obligation mode for the primary program goal (shift in how decisions are made, shift in community power, shift in resource allocation patterns). Use Build obligation mode for the individual funded projects that result from the participatory process (a community center funded through a PB vote, a capacity-building grant allocated by a participatory review panel). The participatory process itself is a Change deliverable; the funded projects it produces carry their own obligation modes.

**Sub-type C (racial equity and power-building):** Use Change obligation mode for systemic change goals: power-building, policy change, narrative shift, reduction in measured racial disparity. Use Build obligation mode for capacity and infrastructure grants to community organizations where the deliverable is organizational capacity (staffing, technology, operational systems) rather than a measurable community condition change.

Trust-Based Philanthropy's default of multi-year unrestricted general operating support creates a tension with CROSS's indicator-based obligation structure. This tension is resolved through Retroactive obligation mode: the funder and grantee agree that the grantee will document and report on uses and outcomes after the fact, with the reporting framework agreed upon at the entry gate but not used to condition disbursement mid-grant. A sufficiency gap declaration must be filed if the grantee and funder agree that outcome measurement in the Change mode is not yet achievable for the funded work.

**Sub-type D (indigenous and community-led development):** Use Change obligation mode where the outcome is community self-determination, sovereignty, or a specific measurable condition defined by the community. The community's own definition of the desired change is the outcome specification; the entry gate must document the process by which the community defined the change they seek, not only what the funder believes the outcome should be.

### Entry Specification Gate

Before any application window opens, before any community vote is held, or before any community partner is designated, the following must be committed to in writing in a location accessible to all potential participants:

**Program specification:**
- Obligation mode (Build, Change, or Retroactive with sufficiency gap declaration)
- Total funding pool and allocation method (participatory review panel, community vote, formula, or designated)
- Eligible grant categories and geographic scope
- Eligibility criteria for applying organizations or community projects
- Outcome indicators that will be tracked, disaggregated by race, income, and geography where applicable
- For Sub-type A: the community decision-maker selection process, the weight of community voice in allocation decisions, and the process for resolving disagreements between community and funder judgment

**Participatory mechanism specification (Sub-types A and B):** The participatory mechanism must be specified before any community member is invited to participate. This is OECD Citizen Participation Guidelines (2022) Principle 1: design the participation mechanism before issuing the invitation. The specification must cover: who is eligible to participate as a decision-maker (selection criteria, demographic targets), how decision-makers are identified and recruited, what authority decision-makers have (full allocation authority, recommendation authority, or a weighted input into a funder decision), the deliberation process (how information about proposals is shared, how discussion is structured, how decisions are reached), and what happens if consensus is not achieved.

**FPIC pre-condition (Sub-type D):** For programs targeting indigenous communities or communities that have explicitly asserted FPIC rights, evidence of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent to the program design is a pre-condition for opening the entry specification gate. The FPIC process must precede the gate, not follow it. The entry gate record must include: the date on which the consent process was initiated, the community bodies or representatives involved, the process by which consent was sought, the outcome of the consent process (full consent, conditional consent with modifications, or non-consent), and any modifications to the program design that resulted from the consent process. An entry gate that opens before FPIC documentation is on record does not satisfy Sub-type D requirements.

**Disaggregation commitment:** The entry gate must specify the disaggregation dimensions for all outcome indicators. For programs applying NCRP racial equity criteria, race and geography are statutory disaggregation dimensions: once collected at this level, data cannot be reported at a less disaggregated level in subsequent periods without disclosure. Income disaggregation is required for programs targeting low-income communities. Disability status, gender, age, and indigenous community membership are required disaggregation dimensions for programs where these characteristics are relevant to the Theory of Change.

**Concurrent funding disclosure:** Programs in this type frequently blend government, foundation, and community-raised funds. All funding sources covering the same scope must be disclosed:
- Government grants or contracts funding the same community partner or geographic area for the same or overlapping purposes
- Foundation grants from other funders covering the same scope
- Community fundraising campaigns or crowdfunding that counts toward program funding totals
- For trust-based general operating support: all operating support sources and their terms, to assess whether the unrestricted grant is genuinely additive

**Causality stance:** Power-building, narrative change, and policy influence are outcome indicators for which attribution is rarely achievable. The entry gate must declare the causality stance for each outcome indicator: contribution for outcomes where the grant is one of multiple contributing factors and a contribution pathway can be articulated, or association for outcomes where the grant's role in the outcome is documented but not structurally causal. Programs claiming attribution for policy change outcomes without a pre-specified independent evaluation plan do not satisfy this field.

### Application Gate

**Organizational identity for grantee partners:** The applying community organization must declare: legal name and registration number, jurisdiction of registration or community governance status for unincorporated community organizations, primary service geography and target community, primary contact and authorized representative, and whether the organization is community-owned, community-controlled, or community-led (defined by whether the governing board, staff leadership, or membership is majority from the communities served).

**Community accountability mechanism:** Each applicant must declare how the grant-funded program will be accountable to the community it serves, distinct from accountability to the institutional funder. Community accountability mechanisms include: community advisory boards with defined authority, community feedback processes with stated response requirements, participatory evaluation with community members as evaluators, and public reporting to the community in accessible formats. A mechanism that routes all accountability through the institutional funder without a direct community feedback channel does not satisfy this field.

**Theory of Change for power-building and systemic change (Sub-type C):** Applications for power-building grants must map the funded activities to power-building outputs (organizing membership growth, leadership development, coalition building) and from outputs to power-building outcomes (policy wins, narrative shift metrics, community decision-making authority gained) and from outcomes to the goal (reduced racial disparity, increased community self-determination, systemic policy change). The Theory of Change must specify which activities are expected to produce which outputs, with the causal narrative at each level transition.

**General operating support use declaration (trust-based grants):** For unrestricted general operating support grants, the applicant declares the anticipated uses of the funds across operating budget categories (staffing, operations, programs, advocacy, evaluation), without being required to commit to specific line-item allocations. The declaration is informational, not a compliance requirement; disbursement is not conditioned on adherence to the declared uses. The use declaration exists to satisfy the entry gate record requirement, not to restrict the grantee.

### Completion Gate

**Participatory process completion (Sub-types A and B):** Completion evidence for the participatory process is the process record: who participated, at what demographic representativeness, through what deliberation structure, and with what decision outcome. For participatory budgeting: the PB vote record, the winning projects, and the funds allocated. For participatory grantmaking: the panel composition, the decisions made, and the allocation record. Process completion is confirmed separately from outcome completion; both must be addressed.

**Outcome indicator data (Sub-types C and D):** Completion evidence for Change obligation outcomes is data from the measurement sources declared at the entry gate, disaggregated at the dimensions committed to at the entry gate. For power-building outcomes: organizing membership counts, policy wins documented with dated evidence, narrative shift metrics from the measurement method declared. For FPIC programs: the community's own assessment of whether the program achieved the changes the community defined at the entry gate.

**Trust-based general operating support (Sub-type C):** For unrestricted grants using Retroactive obligation mode, the completion record is the grantee's narrative report on how funds were used and what outcomes were observed, submitted after the grant period. The report is not a compliance check; it is an attestation corpus document. The funder reviews the report for completeness, not for conformance to a pre-specified use plan.

**Disaggregation confirmation:** At completion, all outcome data must be reported at the disaggregation level committed to at the entry gate. Data reported only at aggregate levels when disaggregated data was committed to at the entry gate constitutes a failure to satisfy the disaggregation ratchet and must be disclosed as a data limitation with an explanation.

**Community decision-maker feedback (Sub-types A and B):** For participatory programs, the completion gate includes a feedback record from community decision-makers about their experience of the process: whether they had sufficient information to make decisions, whether the process was accessible and inclusive, whether the outcome reflected their deliberation, and whether they would recommend the process to other community members. This feedback record is an attestation corpus document, not a performance metric.

### Attestation Gate and Corpus

The Attestation Corpus for participatory and equity-focused grantmaking must include both the funder's records and, for Sub-type D programs, a record created by the community:

- Program specification: published before the community decision-maker selection process begins; includes the FPIC consent documentation for Sub-type D programs
- Application record: stored with the community decision-maker selection and recruitment record
- Participatory process record: dated documentation of each deliberation session, participant attendance, and decision record
- Completion gate determination: the funder's determination and, for Sub-type D, the community's assessment of the program
- Disbursement record: payment amounts and dates
- Disaggregated outcome data: reported at the disaggregation level committed to at the entry gate, with any data limitation disclosures attached
- Community feedback record: the feedback from community decision-makers and, for Sub-type D, from the community about the program's impact on self-determination

For programs reporting under NCRP criteria: the corpus must be structured to produce the data required for NCRP verification. For programs reporting under CRS ProPack, World Vision LEAP, or Core Humanitarian Standard Commitment 5: the community accountability mechanism record must satisfy those standards' beneficiary feedback documentation requirements.

### Continuation Gate

For multi-year programs or programs that renew annually:

A continuing grantee must demonstrate: completion of prior year obligations at the agreed level, an updated community accountability mechanism record showing that community feedback was received and responded to, and updated disaggregated outcome data. For trust-based unrestricted grants: the grantee's narrative report is the continuation evidence; no additional reporting requirements are imposed.

A continuing program operator must demonstrate: the entry specification gate for the new cycle is published before the prior cycle closes, the participatory process specification is updated to reflect any design changes recommended by community decision-maker feedback, and the FPIC consent record is updated if the program has materially changed since the original consent was sought.

## Part 2: WALKRI Field Specifications

The following field specifications cover the intake fields most commonly required in participatory and equity-focused grant programs. Each specification satisfies WALKRI's five pre-publication requirements: criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, compliance threshold.

---

### Field: Community Decision-Maker Role and Selection Process

**Criterion intent:** Establishes how community members are identified, recruited, and empowered as grant reviewers, panel members, or fund allocators, so that the participatory grantmaking mechanism is transparent, the community decision-maker selection is accountable to the communities being served, and the authority of community decision-makers is unambiguous before anyone is invited to participate.

**Operational definition:** A community decision-maker is a person who participates in the allocation decision for a grant program and who is from or representative of the communities the grant program serves, not a representative of the institutional funder or grantmaking foundation. The selection process is the method by which eligible community members are identified, recruited, and confirmed as panelists or allocators. The role specification describes what authority the community decision-maker has: whether their determination is final, whether it is a recommendation that can be overridden, or whether it is a weighted input into a combined funder-community decision.

**Response form:** Selection criteria: who is eligible to serve (geographic, demographic, or community relationship criteria). Recruitment method: how eligible community members are identified and invited (open application, nomination, representative body selection, or random selection from an eligible pool). Compensation: whether community decision-makers are compensated for their time, and at what rate, to ensure that participation is not limited to those with discretionary time. Role authority: a clear statement of whether the community decision-maker's determination is binding on the funder, advisory with stated weight, or one factor in a multi-stakeholder decision with the funder retaining final authority. Conflict of interest policy: how conflicts between a community decision-maker and an applicant are identified and managed.

**Evidence form:** The selection process documentation published before community decision-makers are recruited. At completion: the list of community decision-makers by role (anonymized if requested by participants), their demographic characteristics in aggregate, the compensation paid, and any conflicts identified and how they were managed. If the community decision-maker authority was advisory rather than binding: documentation of any instances where the funder's determination differed from the community decision-maker panel's recommendation, with the reason for the difference.

**Compliance threshold:** A stated selection process with criteria, a recruitment method, and a role authority specification that is unambiguous about whether community voice is binding or advisory. A program that describes community members as "participating" in grantmaking without specifying their decision-making authority, compensation, or selection process does not satisfy this field. Programs where community members are consulted but do not make or substantially determine allocation decisions are not participatory grantmaking programs under CROSS's definition; they may be community-informed grantmaking programs, which carry different entry specification requirements.

---

### Field: Demographic Representation of Decision-Makers

**Criterion intent:** Specifies the demographic representativeness targets for the community decision-maker panel or participatory process, so that the program can assess whether the people making decisions reflect the people the grants are designed to serve, and so that representativeness is a pre-specified criterion rather than a post-hoc assessment.

**Operational definition:** Demographic representativeness is the degree to which the demographic composition of the community decision-maker group reflects the demographic composition of the target community the grant program serves. Representativeness is assessed on dimensions that are material to the program's equity goals: race and ethnicity, income level, geographic sub-area within the program's service geography, gender, age, disability status, indigenous community membership, and first-generation immigrant status, where these characteristics are relevant to the program's Theory of Change. The representativeness target is the desired composition stated as a percentage or proportion, derived from demographic data about the target community.

**Response form:** For each representativeness dimension relevant to the program: the dimension name, the target community's composition on that dimension (the reference population, with the data source), the target composition for the decision-maker panel on that dimension (stated as a percentage or range), and the minimum acceptable threshold below which the funder will take corrective action (extend recruitment, add additional panel members, or disclose the gap). A narrative explanation of which representativeness dimensions are most material to the program's equity goals and why.

**Evidence form:** At recruitment completion: the actual demographic composition of the community decision-maker panel on each specified dimension, compared to the target. If the actual composition falls below the minimum threshold on any dimension: the corrective action taken or a documented explanation of why corrective action was not feasible. At completion: whether the demographic composition of the panel was maintained throughout the grant cycle (i.e., that panel attrition did not compromise representativeness).

**Compliance threshold:** A named target for at least the race/ethnicity and geography dimensions, with a reference population data source. A program that states only that it "seeks diverse community representation" without specifying target proportions or reference population data does not satisfy this field. For programs applying NCRP racial equity criteria: race/ethnicity and geography representativeness targets are required at the statutory disaggregation level; they cannot be downgraded to qualitative description.

### Field: Racial Equity Outcome Indicators

**Criterion intent:** Specifies the measurable indicators through which the program will track whether racial disparities are being reduced or whether power in communities of color is increasing, so that racial equity is a concrete, measurable program outcome rather than a narrative aspiration.

**Operational definition:** A racial equity outcome indicator is a measure of a condition in a racially defined population that reflects either a disparity between racial groups (the gap measure) or the absolute level of a positive condition in a community of color (the level measure). Gap measures compare conditions between racial groups at the same point in time and track whether the gap narrows over the grant period. Level measures track the absolute improvement of a condition in communities of color without requiring comparison to a reference group. Both measure types are valid; the program must declare which type it is using and why, since the choice affects the causality claim and the measurement design.

**Response form:** For each racial equity outcome indicator: indicator name, indicator type (gap measure or level measure), the racial/ethnic groups for which data will be collected, the unit of measurement, the data source (named administrative data system, named survey instrument, or community-defined measurement process), the baseline value disaggregated by race/ethnicity and geography, the target value with the change expected over the grant period, and the disaggregation dimensions beyond race/ethnicity (income, geography, age, gender) that are also required. A narrative explanation of the pathway from grant activities to the racial equity outcome: why the funded work is expected to move this indicator.

**Evidence form:** At completion: outcome data from the named source, disaggregated by race/ethnicity and geography at minimum, with additional disaggregation dimensions as declared. For gap measures: the gap calculation with both reference groups shown. For level measures: the trend data for the community of color over the grant period. Data that is not disaggregated by race/ethnicity does not satisfy this field even if overall population outcomes improved.

**Compliance threshold:** Each racial equity outcome indicator must name the racial/ethnic groups for which data will be collected, a named data source, and a baseline value disaggregated by race/ethnicity. An indicator that tracks population-level outcomes without racial disaggregation does not satisfy this field as a racial equity outcome indicator. Baseline data collected only at the aggregate population level, without racial disaggregation, must be treated as an absence of baseline; the program must commit to racial-disaggregation baseline collection as a grant deliverable.

### Field: FPIC Documentation

**Criterion intent:** For Sub-type D programs targeting indigenous communities or communities asserting FPIC rights, establishes that the community's Free, Prior, and Informed Consent to the program design was sought and received before the program opened, and documents the consent process and outcome in the attestation corpus.

**Operational definition:** Free, Prior, and Informed Consent is a community's affirmative agreement to a program, policy, or intervention that may affect their rights, lands, territories, or livelihoods, given: freely (without coercion, manipulation, or inappropriate incentives), prior to the program beginning (before any activities commence, and with sufficient time for the community to consider and deliberate), and with full information (the community has received complete and accurate information about the program's purpose, design, potential benefits, and potential harms, in a language and format accessible to the community). Consent is a process, not a single event; ongoing consent may be required as the program evolves.

**Response form:** The community entity or entities from which consent was sought (named governing body, council, assembly, or representative group). The date on which the consent process was initiated. The information provided to the community as part of the consent process (a summary of the program design document, the proposed grant conditions, and any potential harms or risks identified by the funder). The community's deliberation process (how the community considered the information, over what period, and who participated in deliberation). The consent outcome: full consent, conditional consent with modifications made (each modification listed), or non-consent with the reason documented. For programs proceeding with conditional consent: the program modifications made in response to the community's conditions, confirmed by the community as satisfying the conditions.

**Evidence form:** A consent documentation record from the community entity: a signed consent declaration, a council resolution, a community assembly record, or equivalent formal documentation of the community's decision. The consent record must bear a date predating the program's entry specification gate opening. For programs in which ongoing consent is required: consent confirmation records at each major program decision point. The absence of a consent record from the community entity, replaced only by the funder's assertion that consent was obtained, does not satisfy this field.

**Compliance threshold:** A dated consent record from a named community governing entity, predating the program's entry specification gate opening. A funder's statement that "the community was consulted" without a formal consent record from the community entity does not satisfy this field. Programs that open their entry specification gate or application window before FPIC documentation is on record have not satisfied Sub-type D requirements; the sequence cannot be retroactively corrected.

### Field: Power-Building Indicators

**Criterion intent:** Specifies the measurable indicators through which the program will track whether the funded organizing, advocacy, and coalition-building activities are increasing the political, economic, or social power of the target community, distinguishing between activity outputs (number of meetings held, number of members recruited) and power outcomes (policy wins, decision-making authority gained, narrative shift).

**Operational definition:** Power-building indicators are measures of the community's capacity to influence decisions that affect its members, distinguish between three types of power: (1) organized people power (the number of people who are engaged in collective action, measured by organizing membership, leadership development, and mobilization participation); (2) decision-making authority (the formal or informal authority the community holds over decisions affecting it, measured by policy wins, seats on decision-making bodies, or budget control); (3) narrative power (the community's ability to shape public understanding of issues affecting it, measured by media presence, narrative shift surveys, or policy discourse analysis). Each type of power must be measured by a distinct indicator; aggregate power cannot be measured by a single composite index without specifying how the components are weighted and combined.

**Response form:** For each power type relevant to the funded work: indicator name, power type (organized people power, decision-making authority, or narrative power), unit of measurement, measurement method (named instrument or process), baseline value or declaration that baseline collection is a grant deliverable, target value at grant completion, and how the indicator will be collected (by grantee staff, by community members, or by an independent evaluator). A narrative explanation of why the selected indicators are the most direct and meaningful measures of the power-building outcomes the grant funds.

**Evidence form:** At completion: indicator data for each power type declared, collected by the method stated at the entry gate. For policy wins: dated documentation of the policy decision and evidence of the grantee's role in achieving it. For narrative shift: survey data or media analysis from the measurement tool declared. For organizing membership: member records or sign-in logs with demographic data attached. Narrative claims of power-building impact without corresponding indicator data do not satisfy this field.

**Compliance threshold:** At least one indicator per power type that is relevant to the funded work, with a named measurement method and a baseline or baseline collection plan. An indicator list that consists only of activity counts (number of meetings, number of events, number of materials distributed) does not satisfy this field as a power-building indicator set; activity counts may appear separately as output specifications. Indicators without a named measurement method cannot be confirmed at the completion gate.

### Field: Process Equity Metrics

**Criterion intent:** Specifies the metrics through which the participatory process itself will be assessed for equity and accessibility, ensuring that the process produces equitable participation rather than reproducing existing power imbalances within the community or excluding community members with barriers to participation.

**Operational definition:** Process equity metrics are measures of the fairness, accessibility, and representativeness of the participatory process, distinct from the outcomes the process produces. They address three dimensions: (1) participation access (whether all eligible community members had a genuine opportunity to participate, regardless of income, language, disability status, work schedule, or childcare obligations); (2) participation quality (whether all participating community members had substantively equal influence in deliberation, regardless of prior grant experience, education, or relationship to the institutional funder); (3) participation outcome (whether the allocation decisions resulting from the process reflect the deliberated preferences of the participant group rather than the preferences of the most vocal or most connected participants).

**Response form:** For each process equity dimension: the metric name, the unit of measurement, the data collection method, and the target. For participation access: language accessibility (number of languages in which materials were provided and facilitation was available), economic accessibility (whether stipends, childcare, and transportation reimbursement were offered), and geographic accessibility (whether in-person and remote participation options were available). For participation quality: a post-process participant survey including items on whether participants felt their input was heard, whether they had sufficient information to participate meaningfully, and whether the facilitation was neutral. For participation outcome: a comparison between the allocation preferences documented in deliberation and the final allocation decisions made.

**Evidence form:** At completion: participation records disaggregated by the demographic dimensions committed to in the Demographic Representation of Decision-Makers field. Post-process participant survey results (aggregated and anonymized). For any instance where the final allocation differed from the deliberated preference of the majority of participants: documentation of why the difference occurred and whether it triggered the funder override mechanism declared at the entry gate.

**Compliance threshold:** Metrics for all three participation dimensions: access, quality, and outcome. A process equity record that documents only attendance (who came) without addressing participation quality (whether attendees had substantive influence) does not satisfy this field. Participant survey results must be collected and attached to the completion record; programs that did not collect post-process participant feedback cannot satisfy this field retroactively.

### Field: General Operating Support Use Declaration

**Criterion intent:** For trust-based unrestricted general operating support grants, provides a non-binding informational declaration of how the grantee anticipates using the funds, satisfying the CROSS entry gate record requirement without creating compliance conditions that would undermine the unrestricted nature of the grant.

**Operational definition:** A general operating support use declaration is an informational statement by the grantee describing how the unrestricted grant will be used across operating budget categories. The declaration is an anticipatory document: it reflects the grantee's plans at the time of application, not a binding commitment. The grantee is not required to report on adherence to the declared uses, and disbursement is not conditioned on any particular use. The declaration exists to create an entry gate record and to give the funder and grantee a shared starting understanding of the grant's likely impact on the grantee's operations.

**Response form:** Operating budget categories covered by the grant, each with an anticipated allocation percentage (not a dollar commitment): staffing and salaries, operations and overhead, program activities, advocacy and organizing, communications, evaluation and learning, and other (described). The approximate percentage of the grantee's total operating budget that the grant represents. A brief narrative (100-200 words) describing the organizational capacity or program areas the grant is expected to strengthen. An explicit statement that the declaration is informational and non-binding.

**Evidence form:** The use declaration document submitted with the application. At completion (for programs using Retroactive obligation mode): the grantee's narrative report describing how funds were actually used, compared to the anticipated uses declared at entry. If actual uses differed materially from anticipated uses: a brief explanation of the organizational circumstances that led to the change. The narrative report is an attestation corpus document, not a compliance determination; the funder does not assess the report for adherence to the entry declaration.

**Compliance threshold:** An anticipated use declaration covering at least the major operating budget categories, with an explicit non-binding statement. A grantee that refuses to provide any use declaration, even informational, cannot satisfy this field; a declaration that an organization's uses cannot be anticipated is itself a compliant response if accompanied by a brief explanation of the organizational circumstances. Programs that use the use declaration to create de facto compliance conditions (e.g., by conditioning renewal on use conformance) have converted a general operating support grant into a restricted grant and must reclassify the grant and apply the corresponding CROSS obligation mode.

### Field: Community Accountability Mechanism

**Criterion intent:** Establishes how the funded program is directly accountable to the community it serves, distinct from accountability to the institutional funder, ensuring that community members have a structured channel to provide feedback on the program and that the program is obligated to respond.

**Operational definition:** A community accountability mechanism is a structured process through which community members can provide feedback on the funded program and receive a documented response from the program operator. The mechanism is direct: it does not route exclusively through the institutional funder. It is structured: feedback is collected through a named process with a defined frequency, format, and response obligation, not only through informal channels. It is responsive: the program operator is obligated to acknowledge feedback and describe what action, if any, was taken in response. Community accountability mechanisms include: community advisory boards with defined authority and meeting frequency, community feedback surveys with stated response requirements, community member participation in program evaluation, and public reporting to the community in accessible formats with an opportunity for community response.

**Response form:** Mechanism type (community advisory board, community survey, participatory evaluation, public reporting with response period, or combination). Frequency of feedback collection (quarterly, semi-annually, annually, or event-triggered). Format and language accessibility of the feedback process. The program's response obligation: within what time period, in what format, and by whom. How feedback will be documented and included in the attestation corpus. For programs serving indigenous communities under Sub-type D: how the community accountability mechanism relates to the FPIC ongoing consent process.

**Evidence form:** At entry: the community accountability mechanism design document or the relevant section of the program design. At completion: the community feedback collected during the grant period (aggregated and anonymized), the program's documented responses to the feedback, and any program changes made in response to community feedback. A narrative describing instances where community feedback led to a program design change, or a statement that no design changes were made and the reason.

**Compliance threshold:** A named mechanism type with a stated frequency, a documented response obligation, and a named person or role responsible for managing the mechanism. A program that relies entirely on informal community relationships as its accountability mechanism, without a structured feedback channel, does not satisfy this field. Programs where community accountability mechanisms exist in the grantee's general operations but are not specified in relation to the funded program do not satisfy this field; the mechanism must be explicitly connected to the grant-funded activities.

## Part 3: Compatibility Map

This section maps the participatory and equity-focused grantmaking program type to the frameworks in the CROSS+WALKRI compatibility corpus that are most directly applicable. The map is organized by primitive.

---

### Pre-specification Primitive

The pre-specification primitive requires that metrics, criteria, and participation structures be committed to before any community member is invited to participate or any applicant submits. Participatory and equity frameworks that implement this primitive:

- **Participatory Budgeting 15 Key Metrics (PB Project)**: process metrics (number of participants, demographic representativeness) and outcome metrics (projects funded, funds allocated) are both declared before any voting or deliberation begins; the pre-specification principle is embedded in the PB design standards
- **OECD Citizen Participation Guidelines (2022)**: the first principle is that participation mechanisms must be designed before any citizen is invited to participate; the participation design is the entry specification, not the participation itself
- **NCRP Racial Equity Criteria**: criteria for equity-focused grantmaking are published before applications open; the disaggregation requirement applies to the entry gate specification, not only to completion reporting
- **CROSS itself**: the entry specification gate is the canonical implementation; for participatory programs, the gate applies to the participation mechanism as well as to the grant criteria

Compatibility statements: PB-Project-15-Key-Metrics, OECD-Citizen-Participation-Guidelines-2022, NCRP-Racial-Equity-Criteria.

### Participatory Decision-Making as a Gate Mechanism

The participatory grantmaking model creates a new gate type not present in standard grantmaking: the participation gate, which confirms that the participatory process was conducted with the specified composition, authority, and deliberative quality before the allocation decisions are recorded. Frameworks addressing this gate:

- **OECD Citizen Participation Guidelines (2022)**: defines participation quality criteria that function as completion criteria for the participation gate; includes representativeness, deliberative quality, and outcome correspondence
- **PB Project Standards**: defines the participation gate for participatory budgeting in terms of voting participation rates, demographic representativeness of voters, and correspondence between vote outcomes and project implementation
- **IAF Grassroots Development Framework**: the Inter-American Foundation's framework requires participatory indicator selection by community members; the indicator selection process is itself a participation gate that must be completed before the grant measurement design is finalized

Compatibility statements: OECD-Citizen-Participation-Guidelines-2022, PB-Project-Standards, IAF-Grassroots-Development-Framework.

### Disaggregation Primitive

The disaggregation primitive requires that outcome data be collected and reported at the disaggregated level relevant to the program's equity claims. For participatory and equity programs, disaggregation by race, income, and geography is at the statutory level of the disaggregation ratchet. Frameworks that address this primitive:

- **NCRP Racial Equity Criteria**: the most detailed disaggregation specification in the philanthropic sector; requires race and geography disaggregation at the field level of the WALKRI specification
- **Equitable Evaluation Initiative (EEI)**: the EEI principles require pre-specifying the disaggregation dimensions before data collection begins, so that equity analysis is built into the evaluation design rather than applied post-hoc to aggregated data
- **SNAP-Ed Equity Lens**: the USDA's nutrition education evaluation framework includes an equity lens requiring demographic disaggregation of all outcome indicators; the framework provides a worked example of how to pre-specify disaggregation dimensions in a government program context
- **IFC OPIM**: disaggregation by gender and vulnerability status is required for all beneficiary counts in IFC-financed programs; this is the most widely applied statutory disaggregation requirement in international development finance

Compatibility statements: NCRP-Racial-Equity-Criteria, Equitable-Evaluation-Initiative, SNAP-Ed-Equity-Lens, IFC-OPIM.

### Beneficiary Accountability Primitive

The beneficiary accountability primitive requires that communities served by grant programs have a structured mechanism to provide feedback to the program and that the program is obligated to respond. Frameworks that implement this primitive:

- **CRS ProPack (Catholic Relief Services)**: defines community feedback and response mechanisms (CFRMs) as required program design elements for all humanitarian and development programs; provides operational guidance on mechanism design, frequency, accessibility, and response obligations
- **World Vision LEAP (Learning through Evaluation with Accountability and Planning)**: requires a Community-Based Feedback and Response Mechanism (CBFRM) for all World Vision programs; the mechanism must be designed before the program begins and must be accessible to all community members, including those with literacy or language barriers
- **Core Humanitarian Standard (CHS) Commitment 5**: all organizations working in humanitarian contexts must establish feedback mechanisms through which affected people can report complaints and receive responses; Commitment 5 specifies that the mechanism must be known to all affected people, accessible without cost, and managed by a named person with response authority
- **Community Foundation National Standards**: the Council on Foundations' national standards for community foundations include community accountability requirements that align with this primitive

Compatibility statements: CRS-ProPack, World-Vision-LEAP, Core-Humanitarian-Standard-Commitment-5, Community-Foundation-National-Standards.

### Theory of Change Primitive

For participatory and equity programs, the Theory of Change must address both the standard activities-outputs-outcomes-goals hierarchy and the distinctive change pathway for power-building and systemic change. Frameworks addressing this primitive:

- **Annie E. Casey Foundation / Results-Based Accountability (RBA)**: RBA distinguishes population accountability (tracking the community condition the program is designed to change) from performance accountability (tracking what the program produced and delivered); this maps directly to CROSS's Change vs. Build obligation mode distinction and to the distinction between power-building outcome indicators and organizing output indicators
- **FSG Collective Impact**: the Collective Impact framework's theory of change requires specifying the conditions for collective success and the shared measurement system, including disaggregation; relevant for participatory and equity programs that involve multiple funders and organizations working toward a common goal
- **NCRP power-building theory of change**: NCRP's grantmaking framework specifies a theory of change for power-building that maps organizing activities to organizing outputs to power outcomes to systemic change goals; this is the most specific Theory of Change specification in the equity grantmaking corpus

Compatibility statements: Annie-E-Casey-RBA, FSG-Collective-Impact, NCRP-Power-Building.

### Independent Verification Primitive

For participatory and equity programs, independent verification often takes the form of participatory evaluation rather than external expert review. Frameworks addressing this:

- **NCRP Criteria verification**: NCRP's verification process involves peer review by other equity-focused funders, not external audit; this is a community-of-practice verification model
- **Equitable Evaluation Initiative pre-publication participation principle**: the EEI requires that the communities being evaluated participate in evaluation design, data collection, data interpretation, and use; the independent evaluator works alongside, not separate from, the community
- **IAF Grassroots Development Framework (participatory evaluation)**: the IAF framework requires that evaluation indicators be selected by community members, that data be collected with community participation, and that evaluation findings be shared with and validated by the community before they are reported to the funder

Compatibility statements: NCRP-Criteria-Verification, Equitable-Evaluation-Initiative, IAF-Grassroots-Development-Framework.

### New Primitive: FPIC as Entry Gate Pre-Condition

FPIC as a pre-condition for opening the entry specification gate is a new primitive identified in Sub-type D programs. No existing framework in the CROSS+WALKRI compatibility corpus treats FPIC as a gate pre-condition rather than a program design principle; the canonical treatment of FPIC in the international development literature (ILO Convention 169, UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples) establishes the right and the consent requirements but does not specify how FPIC fits into a grant management gate sequence.

CROSS extends the FPIC principle by requiring that consent documentation be on record as a pre-condition for opening the entry specification gate. This means the consent process is prior to the gate, not part of it; the gate cannot open until consent is confirmed. This sequencing requirement is new in the grant management standards corpus.

Compatibility statements: ILO-Convention-169, UN-DRIP, World-Bank-IPPF (references to FPIC in project design), but no existing framework specifies the gate sequence.
