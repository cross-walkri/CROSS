# India Corporate Social Responsibility Mandate (Companies Act 2013, Section 135) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

India's Companies Act 2013, Section 135, creates the world's first statutory national CSR mandate at scale, requiring qualifying companies to spend a percentage of their average net profits on CSR activities and to report those activities annually to the Registrar of Companies. The 2021 Amendment Rules introduced mandatory independent third-party impact assessment for larger CSR programs. CROSS+WALKRI-conformant programs satisfy the impact assessment, outcome documentation, and Board disclosure requirements established by these rules, producing the standardized evidence record that third-party assessors and regulatory filings require.

The mandate addresses an estimated ten to fifteen billion USD in annual grant-equivalent social investment, making Section 135 one of the largest regulated social spending regimes in the world.

---

## What the India CSR Mandate Is

Section 135 of the Companies Act 2013 requires every company meeting certain financial thresholds (net worth of one billion rupees or more, or turnover of ten billion rupees or more, or net profit of fifty million rupees or more in the immediately preceding financial year) to constitute a CSR Committee of the Board of Directors, formulate a CSR Policy, and spend at least two percent of average net profits from the preceding three financial years on CSR activities listed in Schedule VII of the Act.

The regime has evolved significantly since its 2014 implementation. The most consequential changes came with the Companies (CSR Policy) Amendment Rules 2021, which:

Made mandatory independent third-party impact assessment for companies whose average CSR obligation over the preceding three years equals or exceeds ten crore rupees (approximately 1.2 million USD), for any project with an outlay of one crore rupees or more that was completed at least one year prior to the assessment. Assessments must be conducted by an independent agency registered with the Ministry of Corporate Affairs (MCA), placed before the Board of Directors, and annexed to the annual report.

Required multi-year project planning: companies are now permitted to undertake CSR projects over multiple financial years, with funds committed in advance, subject to Board approval and registration with the MCA portal.

Introduced Form CSR-2: a standardized annual reporting form filed with the Registrar of Companies, disclosing CSR activities, expenditures, project-level descriptions, outcomes, and impact assessment results. CSR-2 is the primary public accountability instrument for Section 135 compliance.

The MCA has published FAQ guidance clarifying how these requirements apply across different organizational structures, implementing modalities (direct implementation, implementing agency, pooled CSR trust), and project types.

## How CROSS Satisfies the India CSR Mandate

The Section 135 mandate creates three distinct accountability obligations: pre-intervention outcome specification (which is implicit in the impact assessment baseline requirement), during-intervention progress documentation, and post-intervention impact assessment. CROSS's gate architecture satisfies all three.

**Independent third-party impact assessment.** The 2021 Amendment Rules require impact assessment by an independent agency, placed before the Board, and annexed to the annual report. The CROSS independent attestation pathway is the highest-evidence-strength option in CROSS's evidence taxonomy. A completion gate evidence record produced by an independent attestor, following CROSS's gate determination structure, constitutes exactly the evidence form that Section 135 impact assessment requires. Critically, CROSS's entry specification gate produces the pre-specified, baseline-anchored indicator documentation against which impact assessment is measured. Without a pre-specified baseline, third-party assessors must reconstruct the counterfactual, which introduces ambiguity and weakens the assessment's regulatory credibility. CROSS eliminates this problem by requiring baseline specification before disbursement.

**Form CSR-2 outcome documentation.** The CSR-2 form requires companies to document CSR project outcomes in a standardized format, including the project's objectives, the population served, the activities undertaken, the amounts spent, and the outcomes achieved. This maps to CROSS's obligation architecture as follows:

| CSR-2 Required Element | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| Project objectives | Theory of Change entry specification: FROM state, TO state, goal-level declaration |
| Population served | Population scope declaration (Fields covering beneficiary identity, geography, and scale) |
| Activities undertaken | Build-obligation activity specification or change-obligation intervention description |
| Amounts spent | Disbursement records across gate milestones |
| Outcomes achieved | Completion gate evidence record; eleven-field indicator actuals against baseline and target |
| Impact assessment result | Independent attestation record (CROSS highest-evidence-strength pathway) |
| Implementing agency details | Organizational identity declaration; concurrent funding disclosure |

A CROSS-conformant program produces all CSR-2 required elements as structural byproducts of gate conformance. No additional data collection format is required; the CSR-2 form can be populated directly from the CROSS round configuration and gate determination records.

**Board disclosure and public reporting.** The Amendment Rules require that the impact assessment report be placed before the Board of Directors and annexed to the annual CSR report. CROSS's Attestation Corpus, which constitutes a persistently accessible record of all gate determinations and evidential claims, provides the documentation structure for this requirement. The Attestation Corpus is designed to be audit-ready: each gate determination record includes the evidence referenced, the determination made, the standard applied, and the attestor's identity (where applicable). This is the documentary form that Board disclosure and public annexure require.

**Counterfactual baseline requirement.** The 2021 Amendment Rules, as clarified by MCA FAQ guidance, require impact assessments to establish what the situation was before the CSR intervention, not merely what was achieved. This is the counterfactual baseline requirement. CROSS's entry specification gate requires a counterfactual baseline as part of the FROM state declaration for change-obligation programs. The baseline value in Field 10 of CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification is, by definition, the pre-intervention measurement that Section 135 impact assessment requires. A company operating CROSS-conformant CSR programs satisfies the counterfactual baseline requirement automatically, because baseline specification is a gate condition for disbursement.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

Third-party impact assessors working under Section 135 routinely encounter a common failure mode: the CSR program collected data during implementation, but the data collection instruments were designed ambiguously, and the assessor cannot determine whether reported values were produced by a methodology comparable to the baseline measurement. This forces the assessor either to discount the reported values or to substitute their own measurement, which increases assessment cost and reduces the reliability of the regulatory disclosure.

WALKRI addresses this at the source. By requiring that every intake field specify its criterion intent, operational definition, construction methodology, and evidence form before any employee, implementing agency partner, or beneficiary submits data, WALKRI ensures that the measurement methodology is documented and locked before data collection begins. When the third-party assessor arrives, they can read the WALKRI field specification and verify directly whether the reported values are methodologically sound. This shifts impact assessment from interpretive reconstruction to structured verification, which is faster, cheaper, and more defensible in the Board disclosure context.

For companies operating CSR programs through implementing agencies (a common structure under Section 135), WALKRI conformance provides an additional benefit: the implementing agency's data collection instruments are specified to a standard that the company's CSR Committee can evaluate before disbursement, rather than after. This supports the Board's fiduciary obligation to oversee how CSR funds are spent, which Section 135 and the Amendment Rules explicitly assign to the CSR Committee.
