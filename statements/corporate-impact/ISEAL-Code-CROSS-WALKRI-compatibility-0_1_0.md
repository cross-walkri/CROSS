# ISEAL Code of Good Practice Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Note on Scope

CROSS+WALKRI is not currently an ISEAL member and has not sought ISEAL recognition. This statement documents structural alignment between CROSS+WALKRI and the ISEAL Code of Good Practice as a pathway analysis for potential future recognition. It does not constitute a conformance claim and does not imply that CROSS+WALKRI meets all requirements for ISEAL membership. Organizations using CROSS+WALKRI should not represent this statement as evidence of ISEAL conformance.

---

## Summary

CROSS and WALKRI together address ISEAL Code of Good Practice Part 5 (Monitoring, Evaluation and Learning) at the grant program level. Part 5 requires sustainability systems to define indicators before implementation, collect data systematically, and publish findings publicly. CROSS's obligation architecture implements these three requirements structurally: indicators are locked at the entry specification gate before any applicant submits, gate evidence is collected systematically at defined intervals, and conformance records are structured for public disclosure. Organizations seeking ISEAL recognition for a standards system that includes CROSS+WALKRI as its grant-level measurement infrastructure would reference this structural alignment in their conformance documentation.

---

## What the ISEAL Code Is

The ISEAL Alliance Code of Good Practice for Sustainability Systems is the normative framework governing credible sustainability certification systems. The current version, v1.0 effective March 2024 with v1.1 following in September 2025, establishes requirements across five parts: governance and accountability, standard setting, conformity assessment, impacts, and monitoring, evaluation and learning. ISEAL membership and recognition signal to markets, governments, and civil society that a sustainability standard meets internationally recognized credibility criteria.

ISEAL members include the Forest Stewardship Council, Rainforest Alliance, Fairtrade International, the Marine Stewardship Council, and dozens of other major sustainability certification systems. ISEAL recognition has become a de facto requirement for sustainability standards seeking acceptance by major retail buyers, financial institutions implementing ESG due diligence, and government procurement programs with sustainability requirements.

Part 5 of the ISEAL Code governs how standards systems monitor their own performance, evaluate their impacts, and use findings for learning and improvement. It requires: indicator definition before implementation begins, systematic data collection against those indicators, public disclosure of findings, and use of evidence for adaptive management of the standards system itself. These requirements apply to the standards system as an organization, not to the certified entities within it.

---

## Part 5 Requirement 1: Define Indicators Before Implementation

ISEAL Part 5 requires that indicators for monitoring system performance be defined before the monitoring cycle begins, not selected post-hoc to match available data. This requirement is the precondition for all subsequent monitoring credibility: if indicators are selected after data is collected, the monitoring system cannot distinguish between genuine performance and reporting optimized for available data.

CROSS's entry specification gate is the structural implementation of this requirement at the grant program level. The gate locks the eleven-field indicator specification before any applicant submits work. No indicator can be added, removed, or redefined after the entry gate closes without a formal gate record of the change and its rationale. For a sustainability standards system using CROSS to manage its grant programs (or to structure its own performance monitoring), the entry specification gate provides a verifiable, time-stamped record that indicators were defined before implementation, satisfying Part 5's pre-specification requirement with a formal gate artifact rather than a self-declaration.

WALKRI strengthens this requirement by ensuring that the indicators defined at the entry gate are measurement instruments: they have explicit criterion intent, operational definitions, response forms, evidence forms, and compliance thresholds. ISEAL Part 5 does not specify the internal quality of indicators beyond requiring that they be defined before implementation; WALKRI adds the instrument quality layer that ensures the pre-specified indicators are capable of producing reliable monitoring data.

---

## Part 5 Requirement 2: Collect Data Systematically

ISEAL Part 5 requires that data collection be systematic: it should occur at defined intervals, against the pre-specified indicators, and with documented methodology. Systematic collection distinguishes credible monitoring from anecdotal or opportunistic data gathering.

CROSS's gate architecture provides the structural mechanism for systematic data collection. Each gate in the four-gate sequence has a scheduled date and a defined evidence submission requirement. Gate evidence is collected against the pre-specified indicators (Fields 1-11) at the gate intervals, not on an ad hoc basis. The gate record documents what evidence was submitted, when, and against which indicators. Late submission or evidence insufficiency creates a formal record of the deviation.

For a standards system using CROSS, the gate records across all grant programs in a cohort constitute a systematic dataset: each program has submitted evidence against the same indicator specification, at the same gate intervals, in a format that enables cross-program comparison. This is the operational expression of "collect data systematically" applied to a portfolio of programs.

WALKRI's evidence form requirement ensures that the data collected at each gate has a documented, independent verification pathway. Systematic collection that cannot be independently verified does not satisfy ISEAL's credibility requirements; WALKRI's evidence form requirement is the instrument-layer mechanism that builds independent verifiability into the collection design before data collection begins.

---

## Part 5 Requirement 3: Publish Findings Publicly

ISEAL Part 5 requires that monitoring findings be made publicly available. Public disclosure enables stakeholders, including civil society organizations, affected communities, and market actors, to assess the standards system's performance independently. The disclosure requirement applies to summary findings and to the methodologies used to generate them; it does not typically require publication of raw data.

CROSS obligation records are structured for public disclosure. The obligation architecture is designed around the premise that funding relationships, indicator specifications, and gate outcomes should be publicly verifiable rather than held in private reporting systems. Conformance records produced through CROSS gate processes are publishable artifacts: they document what was specified, what evidence was submitted, and what the gate outcome was, in a format sufficient for an independent reviewer to assess program performance.

A standards system seeking ISEAL recognition would publish its CROSS gate records as the monitoring findings that Part 5 requires to be publicly disclosed. The gate record format satisfies the "methodology documented alongside findings" expectation: the indicator specification (the methodology) and the gate evidence record (the findings) are linked artifacts within the same obligation record.

---

## Parts Outside Scope

ISEAL Code Parts 1 through 4 address governance and accountability, standard setting, conformity assessment, and impacts. These parts govern how a sustainability standards system is governed, how its standards are developed, how certification bodies are accredited, and how broader societal impacts are assessed. These are organizational and governance questions that are outside the specification layer addressed by CROSS and WALKRI. This statement addresses only Part 5, where the structural alignment is direct and documentable.
