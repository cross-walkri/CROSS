# IFC Operating Principles for Impact Management Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

A grant program running CROSS+WALKRI produces compliance with IFC Operating Principles for Impact Management (OPIM) Principles 4 and 6 as a structural consequence of conformance. CROSS's pre-application obligation specification directly implements Principle 4's requirement that expected impact be assessed systematically before commitment. WALKRI ensures the intake fields collecting indicator data are measurement instruments rather than labels, satisfying the evidentiary quality that Principle 6's monitoring requirement depends on.

The nine OPIM principles span the full asset management lifecycle: from strategy and allocation through investment assessment, monitoring, exit, review, and public disclosure. Principles 1 through 3 address portfolio-level strategy and asset allocation; Principle 7 addresses exit decisions based on impact; and Principle 9 addresses public reporting on alignment. These four principles address relationships between an asset manager, its portfolio, and its investors that lie outside the scope of a single grants round. CROSS+WALKRI addresses the principles that operate at the level of a grant program: assessment before commitment (Principle 4), monitoring throughout (Principle 6), and documentation practices that support review and disclosure (Principles 8 and 9 partially).

---

## What the IFC Operating Principles for Impact Management Are

The IFC Operating Principles for Impact Management were launched by the International Finance Corporation in 2019 and have since been adopted by more than 160 signatories, including major development finance institutions, asset managers, pension funds, and foundations. They establish a discipline of impact management across the investment lifecycle: from defining strategic intent through monitoring, exit, and public accountability.

The OPIM are organized as nine principles. Each principle specifies a practice the signatory must follow and a disclosure expectation: signatories publish annual Disclosure Statements describing how they implement each principle, which are subject to independent verification.

The OPIM are notable for requiring that impact management practices be embedded in investment processes rather than bolted on. Principle 4's phrase "systematically" is operationally significant: a signatory cannot satisfy it with ad hoc assessments. The methodology must be documented, consistent, and applied before any investment commitment is made.

## How CROSS and WALKRI Satisfy the IFC Operating Principles

### Principle 1: Define Strategic Intent

Principle 1 requires the manager to define its strategic impact objective, set expectations for impact across the portfolio, and ensure the impact strategy is material to the investment strategy.

CROSS addresses this through its program-level Theory of Change declaration (Part IX-B), which requires the program operator to define the goal, outcomes, outputs, and activities the round is designed to produce, together with the causal assumptions connecting them. The ToC declaration makes the impact objective explicit, specific, and visible to all applicants before they apply.

WALKRI is not directly engaged at this principle: strategic intent is a program-level configuration, not a field specification question.

### Principle 2: Manage Strategic Asset Allocation

Principle 2 requires the manager to consider impact characteristics when making allocation decisions, so that the portfolio as a whole advances the strategic intent declared under Principle 1.

This principle operates at the portfolio level, above the scope of an individual grants round. CROSS's cohort position assessment (Part X) provides relevant support: it situates each applicant relative to others in the cohort, surfacing concentration risks and portfolio gaps. A program operator using the cohort position assessment alongside the ToC declaration can make allocation decisions that reflect impact distribution across the round. Full portfolio-level OPIM compliance, however, requires practices that span multiple rounds and involve capital allocation decisions beyond what CROSS governs.

### Principle 3: Establish Manager Requirements

Principle 3 requires that managers working on behalf of the signatory be required to adopt impact management practices consistent with the OPIM.

This principle addresses the manager relationship: it applies when an asset owner delegates impact management authority to a third-party manager. For grants programs, this maps to the relationship between a funder and a grants administrator. CROSS+WALKRI provides the framework an administrator can implement to demonstrate that the program they operate meets OPIM-compatible standards. A funder requiring OPIM compliance from its grants administrator can specify CROSS+WALKRI conformance as the implementation vehicle.

### Principle 4: Assess Expected Impact Systematically Before Commitment

Principle 4 is the most operationally demanding of the nine. It requires that the manager define a methodology for assessing expected impact, apply it systematically before any commitment is made, and use it to inform the commitment decision. The methodology must identify relevant indicators, establish baselines, set targets, and document assumptions about how the investment will produce the expected impact.

CROSS directly and precisely implements Principle 4. The entire CROSS pre-application gate sequence (Parts III through V) exists to ensure that obligation architecture, indicator specifications, and Theory of Change pathway declarations are all in place before any applicant submits a proposal and before any commitment decision is made.

Specifically:

The obligation mode declaration (Part III) requires the program operator to specify whether applicants will be held to output-level obligations (build-obligation), outcome-level obligations (change-obligation), or retrospective evidence obligations (retroactive-obligation) before applications open. This is the pre-commitment declaration that Principle 4 requires.

The eleven-field indicator specification (Part V) requires that every indicator in the round have a defined name, definition, unit, baseline, target, population scope, data source, collection frequency, responsible party, institutional framework alignment, and causality stance before applicants see the intake form. This is the "defined methodology with indicators and baselines established before commitment" that Principle 4's assessment requirement demands.

The Theory of Change architecture (Part IX-B) requires the program to declare its causal pathway from activity to output to outcome to goal, together with named assumptions, before the round opens. This is the assumptions documentation that Principle 4 requires.

WALKRI complements this by ensuring that the intake fields collecting the baseline and indicator data described in the CROSS specification are measurement instruments. A WALKRI-conformant baseline field specifies the counting rule, the time period, the population boundary, and the evidence the reviewer must verify. This is the methodological quality that Principle 4's "systematically" requires.

### Principle 5: Assess Expected Returns and Likelihood

Principle 5 requires the manager to assess the expected financial returns of each investment and the likelihood that expected impact will be achieved, recognizing that impact and financial risk are connected.

For grants programs, financial return assessment does not apply in the investment sense. The relevant portion of Principle 5 for a grant round is the likelihood assessment: what is the probability that the expected impact will be achieved? CROSS's causality stance primitive (Part IV) addresses this directly. A gate configured at contribution stance requires a documented causal pathway, acknowledged co-factors, and a materiality argument; a gate configured at attribution stance requires counterfactual evidence. Both stances require the program to make explicit claims about likelihood rather than asserting impact without qualification.

The critical assumptions list in the pathway registry (Part IX-B, Section 3) is the structural form of likelihood assessment in CROSS: each assumption is a condition that must hold for the pathway to produce the expected impact. Enumerating the assumptions is the same cognitive act as assessing the likelihood that the impact will occur as planned.

### Principle 6: Monitor Progress of Each Investment

Principle 6 requires tracking actual impact against pre-defined indicators throughout the investment period. The monitoring methodology must be documented, consistent, and applied to each investment in the portfolio.

CROSS directly implements Principle 6 through its gate architecture. Progress verification gates (Part IV, Section 4.2) require the program to collect evidence against the indicators specified in Part V at defined intervals during the grant period. Because the indicators are pre-specified, the monitoring is structured rather than improvised. Because the gate architecture is mandatory for CROSS-conformant programs, the monitoring is applied consistently across all grantees in the round.

The data source and collection frequency fields (Part V, Fields 7 and 8) specify where monitoring data comes from and how often it is collected. These fields are declared before the round opens, satisfying Principle 6's requirement that the monitoring methodology be established in advance.

WALKRI contributes to Principle 6 compliance at the field level. A WALKRI-conformant progress reporting field specifies the evidence form required for each indicator value submitted: not just the number, but the artifact type and access path that proves the number. This makes progress monitoring verifiable rather than self-reported.

### Principle 7: Conduct Exits Based on Impact

Principle 7 requires that exit decisions consider whether impact has been achieved or can be sustained without the investor's involvement, and whether exiting will create unintended negative consequences.

For grants programs, "exit" corresponds to the end of a grant relationship. CROSS's continuation gate (Part IV, Section 4.3) addresses this directly. The continuation gate requires a sustainability stance declaration with three positions: the impact is sustained (the grantee's activities will continue without further funding), conditional (sustained under specified conditions), or dependent (further funding is required for the impact to persist). This sustainability stance is the grants-program equivalent of Principle 7's exit impact assessment.

The unintended outcomes disclosure requirement (Part IV, completion gate) surfaces negative consequences that might accompany an exit, addressing Principle 7's concern about exit causing harm.

### Principle 8: Review, Document, and Improve

Principle 8 requires the manager to review and document lessons learned from impact management practice and use them to improve future practice.

CROSS's Attestation Corpus query (Part VIII) and post-round documentation requirements provide the structural basis for learning documentation. The Attestation Corpus accumulates evidence from prior rounds, making comparative learning across cohorts possible. The gate-level documentation architecture produces a record of what was specified, what was claimed, and what evidence was provided, which is the raw material for the retrospective review Principle 8 requires.

WALKRI's calibration record requirement (Part V, Section 5.4) captures inter-reviewer calibration data over time. This is a direct input to the learning and improvement cycle: patterns in calibration difficulty identify fields that need operational definition revision.

### Principle 9: Publicly Disclose Alignment

Principle 9 requires signatories to publish an annual Disclosure Statement describing how they implement each of the nine principles and to submit to independent verification.

CROSS and WALKRI are not a disclosure format; they are an obligation architecture. However, a program operating CROSS+WALKRI produces the documentation that OPIM Disclosure Statements require to describe implementation of Principles 4 and 6: the indicator specifications, the pre-commitment ToC declarations, the gate configuration, and the evidence standards are all specified and accessible. A program operator seeking OPIM signatory status can reference their CROSS+WALKRI implementation as the documented methodology for Principles 4 and 6.

## Who Should Reference This Compatibility Statement

Development finance institutions, foundations, and asset managers that are OPIM signatories and operate grants programs alongside their investment portfolios. Adopting CROSS+WALKRI for the grants program satisfies Principle 4 and Principle 6 requirements structurally for the grants instrument, producing compliant documentation without additional process overhead.

Program operators seeking OPIM signatory status who use grants as a primary delivery mechanism. CROSS+WALKRI provides the systematic methodology and monitoring documentation that OPIM signatory verification requires.

Co-financing programs between OPIM signatories and other funders. When a CROSS+WALKRI-conformant program is co-financed by an OPIM signatory, the indicator specifications and gate documentation produced by CROSS+WALKRI satisfy the signatory's Principle 4 and 6 reporting requirements for that program.
