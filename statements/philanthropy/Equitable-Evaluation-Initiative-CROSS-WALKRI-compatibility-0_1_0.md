# Equitable Evaluation Initiative (EEF) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

CROSS+WALKRI's structural provisions address four of the Equitable Evaluation Framework's core challenges through design choices that are built into the architecture rather than left to individual evaluator conduct. The EEF's insistence that what counts as evidence, who defines success, and how attribution claims are constructed are not neutral technical choices but political ones is satisfied by CROSS's pre-commitment requirement and contribution-versus-attribution causality stance, and by WALKRI's pre-publication audit requirement that creates space for community participation in evidence design before any applicant encounters a data collection form.

The Equitable Evaluation Initiative (EEI) developed the Equitable Evaluation Framework (EEF) beginning in 2017 in partnership with philanthropy, nonprofit, and evaluation practitioners. The EEF challenges core assumptions of conventional evaluation practice, specifically targeting the ways in which traditional standards of rigor, objectivity, and validity can replicate structural inequities when applied to communities historically underserved or actively harmed by evaluation processes. The EEF is increasingly cited as a normative framework in US foundation evaluation policy and is referenced by major philanthropies including the Robert Wood Johnson Foundation, the W.K. Kellogg Foundation, and the Annie E. Casey Foundation.

---

## The EEF's Approach

The Equitable Evaluation Framework begins from the observation that evaluation is not a neutral process. Decisions about what counts as evidence, which methodologies are considered rigorous, whose knowledge is treated as authoritative, and how causal claims are constructed all reflect assumptions that favor some epistemologies and communities over others. Traditional evaluation practice has frequently treated quantitative measurement as inherently more rigorous than qualitative or community-generated knowledge, required communities to prove impact by standards they had no role in designing, and made attribution claims that erased the complexity of social change by asserting single-actor causality.

The EEF proposes a set of principles to address these patterns. Among the most structurally significant are: that affected communities should participate in defining what counts as evidence of success before data collection begins; that evaluation methods should reflect epistemological pluralism rather than privileging a single standard of rigor; that attribution claims should acknowledge rather than erase structural complexity; and that evaluation findings should be used to strengthen communities rather than to police them.

A central concern of the EEF is the timing of evidence design decisions. In conventional evaluation practice, evaluators often decide retrospectively what evidence is relevant, which creates conditions for selective interpretation. The EEF calls for pre-defining evidence criteria with community participation so that the standards applied to a community's work are ones that community helped create. This is not merely a procedural preference; it is the EEF's primary structural intervention against extractive evaluation practice.

The EEF also challenges evaluation frameworks that impose a single causal logic on complex social change. Many communities working toward structural change operate in contexts where multiple actors, historical conditions, and systemic forces all contribute to observed outcomes. Evaluation frameworks that require communities to claim singular attribution for outcomes, or that treat programs as isolated from the broader ecosystem of change, systematically undercount the contributions of communities doing interconnected work.

The framework's concern about how findings are used reflects a further structural insight: evaluation findings can function as tools of accountability to funders rather than as tools of learning for communities. An equitable evaluation framework must produce findings that are useful to the community being evaluated, not only to the institution holding the checkbook.

## How CROSS Satisfies the EEF

| EEF Principle | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Pre-define what counts as evidence with community participation | Entry specification gate: criteria published before applications open; pre-commitment requirement | Full satisfaction. CROSS's pre-commitment requirement mandates that evaluation criteria be specified and published before any applicant sees the form. This creates the structural window for community review of success definitions that the EEF requires. Criteria set after submissions arrive cannot have been co-designed by the community; criteria set and published in advance can be. |
| Epistemological pluralism: whose knowledge counts as evidence | CROSS evidence taxonomy: accommodates qualitative and quantitative evidence forms without hierarchical preference | Full satisfaction. CROSS does not prescribe an evidence modality hierarchy. WALKRI's evidence form field requires specification of artifact type (narrative, document, quantitative record, community testimony) without requiring that quantitative evidence be preferred. Programs can configure gate requirements to accept community-generated knowledge as primary evidence. |
| Attribution claims that acknowledge structural complexity | Causality stance field (CROSS Indicator Field 11): contribution-versus-attribution declaration required for each indicator | Full satisfaction. CROSS Field 11 requires programs to declare for each indicator whether they are claiming attribution (the program caused this outcome) or contribution (the program contributed to this outcome alongside other factors). Contribution stance is the structurally appropriate default for complex social change contexts. Programs cannot implicitly assume single-actor attribution; they must declare it, and the declaration triggers a corresponding evidence standard. |
| Community participation in defining success before data collection | CROSS entry specification gate pre-round publication requirement: all criteria public before the application window opens | Full satisfaction. The structural window created by CROSS's pre-round publication requirement is the window in which community co-design of success definitions occurs. A grant program using CROSS that opens its entry specification gate criteria to community comment before the application window is implementing the EEF's participatory approach as a consequence of CROSS conformance. |

**Result:** CROSS satisfies the four core EEF structural principles as architectural consequences of conformance. CROSS does not guarantee that a program will engage communities equitably; it creates the structural conditions under which equitable engagement can occur and makes it visible when those conditions are not met.

This distinction is important. The EEF is a normative framework that describes how evaluation should be conducted; it does not itself provide an architecture for ensuring that its principles are followed. CROSS provides that architecture. A program that is CROSS-conformant and engages communities in evidence design during the entry specification gate's pre-round period is implementing the EEF's core principle through a verifiable, auditable process. A program that uses CROSS without community engagement in that window is conformant with CROSS but not with the EEF; the gap is visible precisely because CROSS makes the timing of evidence design decisions transparent.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication audit requirement is the structural implementation of the EEF's core principle that affected communities should participate in defining what counts as evidence before data collection begins. The pre-publication audit creates a mandatory pause between evidence design and evidence collection. During that pause, WALKRI requires that five elements be specified for each intake field: what the field is measuring, how the criterion is bounded, what response format is expected, what artifact constitutes evidence, and what the minimum threshold for compliance is.

This pause is the structural opportunity for community co-design. If a program commits to community participation in evidence design during the WALKRI pre-publication audit stage, then every field an applicant encounters has been through both a measurement specification review and a community legitimacy review. The resulting intake form is not a funder's unilateral statement of what success looks like; it is a co-produced measurement instrument that communities helped design.

WALKRI also addresses the EEF's concern about retrospective evidence decisions. Because WALKRI requires evidence form specification before the form is published, reviewers cannot decide after submissions arrive that a different kind of evidence would have been more appropriate. The evidence criteria are locked before data collection begins. This is the structural analog of the EEF's call to pre-define evidence standards: not as a procedural nicety but as a protection against the retrospective rationalization that allows funder preferences to masquerade as methodological rigor.

For US foundations referencing the EEF in their evaluation policies, CROSS+WALKRI provides a concrete implementation architecture that makes EEF principles auditable rather than aspirational.
