# NCRP Criteria for Philanthropy at Its Best and Racial Equity Grantmaking

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

Two complementary published frameworks address equity and accountability in philanthropic practice: the National Committee for Responsive Philanthropy (NCRP) "Criteria for Philanthropy at Its Best" (2009) and the Philanthropic Initiative for Racial Equity (PRE) "Grantmaking with a Racial Justice Lens." CROSS+WALKRI satisfies both frameworks' structural requirements by generating the disaggregated, pre-committed data foundations each framework needs. This statement maps the specific provisions of each to the corresponding CROSS and WALKRI mechanisms.

---

## The Frameworks' Approach

The NCRP "Criteria for Philanthropy at Its Best" is notable in the philanthropy landscape for doing something most accountability frameworks avoid: it specifies concrete percentage thresholds. Funders meeting the criteria are expected to direct at least 50% of grant dollars to benefit lower-income communities, communities of color, and other marginalized groups. At least 25% of grant dollars should support advocacy, organizing, and civic engagement. At least 50% of grants should be general operating support rather than project-restricted funding. At least 50% of grants should be multi-year.

These thresholds operate at the portfolio level, not the individual grant level. A single grant program cannot self-certify as NCRP-aligned by examining only one grant; compliance requires examining the distribution of funding decisions across an entire portfolio and measuring that distribution against population need benchmarks. This makes the NCRP criteria fundamentally a data collection and aggregation challenge: funders must have population-disaggregated information about who receives funding and under what conditions, organized in a form that supports portfolio-level analysis.

The PRE "Grantmaking with a Racial Justice Lens" takes a different analytical approach. Rather than specifying thresholds, it equips practitioners to identify how anti-Black racism and white supremacy operate within grantmaking practice itself, including in criteria setting, portfolio composition decisions, and the design of reporting requirements. A key distinction the PRE framework draws is between racial equity (reducing measured disparities between groups) and racial justice (addressing the structural conditions that produce and reproduce those disparities). Grantmaking that pursues only equity, without naming or addressing structural causes, remains incomplete in the PRE framework's analysis.

The two frameworks are complementary rather than overlapping. NCRP provides measurable portfolio benchmarks; PRE provides a structural analysis of the conditions those benchmarks exist within. Together they form a practical and theoretical accountability system for equitable philanthropy.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Frameworks

CROSS provides the structural data architecture that both frameworks require but do not themselves specify how to implement.

The NCRP portfolio composition requirements depend on the funder having reliable, comparable, longitudinal information about who receives funding. CROSS's Cohort Position assessment addresses this directly. Before final gate determinations are made in any round, CROSS requires an analysis of how applicants are distributed across population categories defined in the round specification. This creates a structured data foundation for measuring portfolio composition against NCRP thresholds across rounds and across time. Because the population categories must be declared before the application window opens (as part of the round specification pre-commitment), retrospective category creation to justify portfolio decisions is not possible.

CROSS's population scope declaration and disaggregation ratchet work together to produce the longitudinal demographic data that NCRP portfolio analysis requires. Once a population is specified as within scope for a program, and once demographic data about that population is collected in a disaggregated form, the ratchet provision means those disaggregation categories cannot be dropped in subsequent rounds. This prevents the common failure mode in which demographic tracking begins with apparent rigor but is quietly discontinued when results are unfavorable or when program leadership changes.

The PRE racial justice requirement that programs acknowledge structural context rather than claiming single-actor attribution maps to CROSS's contribution-versus-attribution causality stance field. Programs using CROSS declare in their indicator specifications whether they are claiming to have caused an outcome or contributed to it alongside other structural factors. This is the formal mechanism for encoding the acknowledgment the PRE framework requires: that outcomes involving systemic inequity reflect multiple structural causes, and that no single program can claim exclusive causal responsibility. The PRE framework identifies single-actor attribution as itself a symptom of white supremacist logic in grantmaking practice; CROSS's causal stance field provides the structural form for recording the alternative.

| NCRP/PRE Requirement | CROSS Provision |
|---|---|
| Portfolio composition data (population categories) | Population scope declaration + pre-commitment |
| Longitudinal demographic tracking | Disaggregation ratchet |
| Distribution analysis before final decisions | Cohort Position assessment |
| Structural causality acknowledgment | Contribution-vs-attribution causal stance field |
| Anti-attribution for systemic outcomes | Indicator causal modifier in Fields 6-9 |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication audit ensures that the intake fields collecting equity and population data function as genuine measurement instruments rather than labeling exercises. A field that asks applicants to identify as serving "communities of color" without specifying which communities, how that determination is made, and what evidence is required to support the claim produces data that cannot support NCRP portfolio analysis. WALKRI's audit procedure verifies that each field has an operational definition specifying what information is collected, how it will be verified, and what measurement purpose it serves.

This matters for both frameworks. For NCRP, it means that population-disaggregated data collected through WALKRI-conformant intake is trustworthy enough to compare across rounds and to report against threshold benchmarks. For PRE, it means that equity categories built into intake forms are grounded in specified operational definitions rather than in the implicit assumptions of program designers, which is itself a form of the structural analysis PRE calls for.
