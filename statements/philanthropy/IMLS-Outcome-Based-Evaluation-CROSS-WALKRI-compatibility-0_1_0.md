# IMLS Outcome-Based Evaluation

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Institute of Museum and Library Services Outcome-Based Evaluation (IMLS OBE) framework, mandated under the Government Performance and Results Act (GPRA) for all IMLS grantees, requires five structural elements for every grant: intended outcomes, indicators, data sources, targets for change, and timing. These five elements map directly to CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification. IMLS OBE's definition of outcomes as "benefits to people" explicitly excludes activities and outputs, producing the same structural distinction that CROSS enforces through the Theory of Build gate. WALKRI's pre-publication requirements apply to IMLS intake instruments to ensure that the five structural elements are specified before any applicant submits a proposal, converting the OBE framework from a post-application documentation requirement into a pre-specified commitment architecture.

---

## The IMLS Outcome-Based Evaluation Framework

The Institute of Museum and Library Services administers federal grant programs for museums and libraries under the Museum and Library Services Act (20 U.S.C. § 9101 et seq.) and is required under GPRA to demonstrate performance results for all funded activities. IMLS operationalizes this requirement through its Outcome-Based Evaluation framework, which is mandatory for all IMLS grant programs and supported by the Shaping Outcomes online curriculum developed to help grantees implement OBE correctly.

IMLS OBE requires five structural elements in every grant application and final report. Intended Outcomes are defined as benefits to people, specifically changes in skill, knowledge, attitude, behavior, condition, or life status. This definition is carefully bounded: activities (what the grantee does) and outputs (what the grantee produces) are explicitly excluded. Indicators are the observable, measurable evidence that an outcome is occurring. Data Sources specify who will be measured and what data will be collected. Targets for Change are quantified expectations, stated before the grant period, such as "75% of participating parents will demonstrate increased understanding." Timing specifies the pre/post measurement design through which the change will be detected.

IMLS provides substantial implementation support through Shaping Outcomes, an online curriculum that walks grantees through the logic of outcome thinking and the construction of each of the five elements. Despite this support, the primary failure mode in IMLS OBE implementation is specification at the wrong level of abstraction: grantees state intended outcomes in terms close enough to activities or outputs that no measurement instrument can reliably detect the claimed change in participants.

## How CROSS Satisfies the IMLS OBE Framework

CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification satisfies the five IMLS OBE structural elements at a structural level, providing a more granular specification architecture than OBE requires while fully satisfying each of OBE's five requirements.

**Intended Outcomes and Theory of Change:** IMLS OBE's definition of outcomes as benefits to people, explicitly excluding activities and outputs, corresponds precisely to CROSS's Theory of Change hierarchy. CROSS requires that programs distinguish activities, outputs, outcomes, and goals before any application opens; the completion gate accepts only outcome-level evidence where outcome evidence is required, and rejects output-only claims in that tier. A CROSS-conformant IMLS grantee cannot satisfy the completion gate by reporting activities or outputs in place of the participant-level changes that constitute IMLS OBE's intended outcomes.

**Five OBE elements in CROSS field mapping:** IMLS Indicators (observable, measurable evidence) correspond to CROSS Fields 1, 2, and 3: the indicator name, its definition, and its unit of measurement, all of which must be specified before applications open. Targets for Change correspond to Field 5 (target): the quantified expected change against which completion is assessed. Timing corresponds to Field 6: the temporal specification of when data is collected relative to the intervention. Data Sources correspond to Field 7 (data source) and Field 8 (collection method), which together specify who is measured and by what protocol. CROSS's Fields 9 through 11 (comparison group, causality stance, evidence threshold) extend beyond OBE's five elements, providing the additional specification needed for CROSS's causality gate requirements.

**Entry specification gate and pre-application commitment:** IMLS OBE requires these five elements in grant applications, which means they are specified after the grant program's intake instruments have already been designed. CROSS requires equivalent specification before the intake instruments open, transforming OBE's documentation requirement into a pre-commitment that applicants respond to rather than construct.

| IMLS OBE Element | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| Intended Outcomes (benefits to people, not activities or outputs) | Theory of Change hierarchy; completion gate outcome evidence requirement |
| Indicators (observable, measurable) | Fields 1-3 (name, definition, unit) specified at entry gate |
| Targets for Change (quantified pre-grant expectations) | Field 5 (target) specified at entry gate |
| Timing (pre/post measurement design) | Field 6 (timing) specified at entry gate |
| Data Sources (who measured, what data collected) | Fields 7-8 (data source, collection method) specified at entry gate |
| Output exclusion from outcome claims | Theory of Build gate; completion gate rejects output-only evidence for outcome indicators |
| Shaping Outcomes curriculum alignment | CROSS Build obligation mode structured coaching requirement |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements apply to IMLS intake instruments to ensure that the five OBE structural elements are fully specified before any applicant encounters the form. Without WALKRI-level pre-publication specification, the most common failure mode is that OBE elements are nominally present in the application template but insufficiently defined to constrain applicant responses. An IMLS intake field that asks for "intended outcomes" without specifying what counts as an observable, measurable change in participant skill, knowledge, or behavior leaves the specification problem to the applicant and guarantees heterogeneous outcome claims that cannot be compared across a grant cohort.

WALKRI's operational definition requirement, applied before any intake field is deployed, resolves this at source. For an IMLS library program, the operational definition field specifies what counts as "demonstrated increased understanding" before any applicant writes their proposal: what assessment instrument, what score threshold, what administration timing. WALKRI's evidence form requirement specifies what documentation type satisfies the data source element: observation protocol, pre/post survey, skill demonstration, or administrative record. WALKRI's compliance threshold requirement specifies the minimum target for change that the program will accept as evidence of outcome achievement, converting the OBE Targets for Change element from a grantee-defined variable into a pre-specified program commitment.
