# Trust-Based Philanthropy Project

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Trust-Based Philanthropy Project (trustbasedphilanthropy.org), developed from the Whitman Institute's practice and formalized as a multi-foundation initiative from 2020, defines six core practices for equitable grantmaking. CROSS+WALKRI's structural requirements align with each of these practices and in several cases provide operational implementations that Trust-Based Philanthropy identifies as desirable but does not itself specify in structural form. This statement maps each practice to the relevant CROSS and WALKRI mechanisms.

---

## The Trust-Based Philanthropy Project's Approach

Trust-Based Philanthropy emerged from a critique of conventional grantmaking's power dynamics. The standard philanthropic relationship places substantial information-production burdens on grantees while funders retain full discretion over decision criteria, application of standards, and outcome of deliberations. Grantees invest significant time producing applications and reports whose standards shift across rounds; they cannot verify whether criteria applied to their application were the criteria stated when they applied; they receive little information about how their feedback on the grant process has been used.

The six core practices address this power asymmetry through both process and relationship changes. Practice 1 (multi-year unrestricted funding) reduces the time grantees spend on re-application cycles. Practice 2 (funder-led due diligence) shifts research and information-gathering from grantees to funders. Practice 3 (simplified and streamlined paperwork) eliminates ambiguity and redundancy in application and reporting forms. Practice 4 (transparent and responsive communication) requires funders to share information about decisions and reasoning, not only final outcomes. Practice 5 (soliciting and acting on grantee feedback) creates accountability for the grant process itself. Practice 6 (sharing power) addresses the structural discretion funders hold over criteria, standards, and evaluation.

The Trust-Based Philanthropy Project has been adopted by a growing number of US and international foundations as an operational benchmark. It is increasingly referenced in foundation strategy documents and in funder learning communities. However, it does not specify structural mechanisms for implementing most of its practices: the question of how a funder demonstrates that paperwork has been genuinely simplified, or that power has been genuinely shared, is left to each foundation's interpretation.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Trust-Based Philanthropy Project

CROSS provides the structural mechanisms that make Trust-Based Philanthropy practices independently verifiable rather than self-reported.

Practice 6 (sharing power with grantees) is the most structural of the six practices, and CROSS addresses it most directly. Sharing power over grant criteria means that the criteria used to evaluate an application are not solely within the funder's discretion to define and apply after submissions arrive. CROSS's pre-commitment requirement specifies that round criteria, eligibility thresholds, gate definitions, and evidence requirements must all be published in a round specification before any application window opens. Once published, this specification is part of the independently verifiable record. A grantee who takes the stated criteria at face value has a documented basis for that reliance. Post-hoc changes to standards that disadvantage applicants who responded to the published criteria are structurally prevented: if a determination departs from the pre-committed specification, that departure must be documented and justified in the gate record, not simply applied silently.

Practice 4 (transparent and responsive communication) and Practice 5 (soliciting and acting on grantee feedback) both require that grantees have access to information about how decisions were made, not only their outcome. CROSS's gate record publication requirement provides this. Determination rationale is documented at each gate and is available to grantees and the public. Because the gate record references the pre-committed specification, grantees can verify whether the criteria applied were the criteria stated. This is the structural form of the transparency and responsiveness Trust-Based Philanthropy calls for.

Practice 2 (funder-led due diligence scaled to program size) is addressed by CROSS's proportionality principle. CROSS does not specify a single fixed evidence standard applied uniformly to all programs regardless of size or stage. Evidence requirements scale to the program type, the obligation mode (Build, Change, or Retroactive), and the gate stage. A small pilot program is not required to produce the same evidentiary record as an organization seeking multi-year continuation funding. This is the structural implementation of the proportionality that Trust-Based Philanthropy identifies as a due diligence principle.

| Trust-Based Philanthropy Practice | CROSS Provision |
|---|---|
| Practice 2: Funder-led due diligence | Proportionality principle (evidence scales to program type) |
| Practice 3: Simplified paperwork | WALKRI pre-publication audit |
| Practice 4: Transparent communication | Gate record publication |
| Practice 5: Act on grantee feedback | Gate record references pre-committed specification |
| Practice 6: Share power | Pre-commitment requirement (criteria published before window opens) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication audit is the structural implementation of Practice 3 (simplified and streamlined paperwork). The audit requires that each field in an intake form be specified before any applicant sees it: what information the field collects, what measurement purpose it serves, and what evidence a grantee must provide to respond adequately. A field that appears clear to a program officer but has an ambiguous operational definition forces grantees to over-produce data, hedging against multiple possible interpretations. WALKRI's audit procedure identifies and resolves that ambiguity before publication.

This matters for the trust relationship as well as the paperwork burden. A grantee responding to a WALKRI-conformant form knows exactly what evidence is required and can provide it efficiently. The information asymmetry that Trust-Based Philanthropy identifies as a power imbalance, in which grantees do not know what funders actually need and funders do not communicate it clearly, is addressed structurally by the requirement to specify fields before any applicant engages with them.
