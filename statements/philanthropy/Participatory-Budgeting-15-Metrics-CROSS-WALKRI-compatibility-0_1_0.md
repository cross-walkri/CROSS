# Participatory Budgeting Project 15 Key Metrics

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Participatory Budgeting Project (participatorybudgeting.org), the North American PB Research Board, and Public Agenda jointly published "15 Key Metrics for Evaluating Participatory Budgeting: A Toolkit for Evaluators and Implementers." The toolkit defines fifteen specific and independently verifiable metrics organized across three domains: civic and political life, inclusion and equity, and transformations of government. CROSS+WALKRI provides the structural architecture that PB processes need to collect and report against these metrics consistently and in a form that supports cross-site comparison.

---

## The Participatory Budgeting Project's Approach

Participatory budgeting (PB) is a democratic process in which community members directly decide how to spend part of a public or organizational budget. The PB Project toolkit was developed in response to a recognized gap: PB had expanded rapidly as a practice, but evaluation was inconsistent. Different implementations measured different things, using different definitions and different methods, making cross-site comparison and field-level learning nearly impossible. The 15 Key Metrics toolkit provides a shared measurement framework specifically for PB.

The three domains address distinct dimensions of what PB is supposed to accomplish. The civic and political life domain measures PB's effects on who participates in democratic processes: voter registration and turnout among PB participants, civic knowledge and capacity development, and the scope and diversity of participation. This domain treats PB as a civic engagement intervention and asks whether it is producing civic effects beyond the immediate budget allocation.

The inclusion and equity domain measures whether PB is genuinely reaching communities that are typically underrepresented in public decision-making. This requires disaggregated data: it is not sufficient to know that ten thousand people participated if those ten thousand are disproportionately drawn from already-resourced and already-engaged communities. The domain requires data on who participates, disaggregated by demographic characteristics, alongside data on which projects receive funding and which communities those projects serve.

The transformations of government domain measures whether PB is changing how institutions operate. This is the most structural of the three domains: it asks whether government agencies and public institutions are becoming more responsive, whether budget allocation patterns are changing in ways that reflect PB outcomes, and whether PB has produced durable changes in institutional behavior rather than a series of episodic consultations.

The toolkit specifies fifteen metrics with defined measurement methods, making each metric independently verifiable rather than self-assessed. This distinguishes the PB toolkit from most civic participation accountability frameworks, which describe desired outcomes without specifying how to measure them.

## How CROSS Satisfies the 15 Key Metrics

CROSS's structural provisions support measurement of all three metric domains, with the most direct mappings in the inclusion/equity and government transformation domains.

Civic participation metrics (who participates, how many, with what civic characteristics) require population-scope-level data collected during the PB process itself. CROSS's population scope declaration specifies the population from which participants are drawn before the process begins. CROSS's disaggregation ratchet ensures that once the process begins collecting disaggregated participation data (by age, geography, language, or other categories), those categories are maintained across subsequent rounds. The longitudinal tracking the PB toolkit's civic metrics require, comparing participation profiles across PB cycles rather than treating each cycle in isolation, is structurally supported by the ratchet provision.

The inclusion and equity domain's requirement for disaggregated data on populations served by funded projects maps to CROSS's outcome indicator disaggregation requirements. In CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification, Fields 8 and 9 require that population specification be included in the outcome indicator: who is expected to experience the outcome, with what demographic characteristics, in what geographic scope. A PB process using CROSS to document its funded projects generates disaggregated, standardized records of which communities the funded projects serve, which is the data the equity metrics require.

The government transformation domain's metrics on institutional responsiveness and changes in institutional behavior map to CROSS's Change obligation mode. Change obligation programs specify at intake what institutional conditions they intend to shift, using condition-level indicators at Fields 7 through 9 of the indicator specification. These indicators measure changes in institutional conditions, not only project outputs: whether a housing agency changed its intake procedures, whether a school allocated resources differently following a PB vote, whether an infrastructure department modified its planning process. This is the measurement structure that government transformation metrics require; it operates at the condition level rather than the output level.

CROSS's gate record publication provides the evidence trail for demonstrating, across PB cycles, that metrics were measured consistently using stated definitions. Because gate records reference pre-committed specifications, evaluators can verify that the metric definitions used in year three of a PB program were the same definitions used in year one, which is a precondition for longitudinal comparison.

| PB Toolkit Domain | CROSS Provision |
|---|---|
| Civic metrics (who participates, longitudinal) | Population scope declaration + disaggregation ratchet |
| Equity metrics (disaggregated population data) | Indicator Fields 8-9 (population specification) |
| Government transformation metrics | Change obligation mode, condition-level indicators |
| Longitudinal metric consistency | Gate record referencing pre-committed specification |
| Cross-site comparability | Standardized eleven-field indicator specification |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI ensures that the intake fields collecting participation and equity data in PB processes are measurement instruments with operational definitions. For the PB toolkit's metrics to be useful, the data underlying them must be collected consistently: "participant" must mean the same thing in round 3 as it meant in round 1, and the demographic categories used to disaggregate participation data must have stable definitions.

WALKRI's pre-publication audit specifies exactly who counts as a participant for each field in the intake form, how disaggregation categories are defined, and what evidence a participant must provide to be counted in a given demographic category. This operational precision is what makes the underlying data trustworthy for metric calculation. Without it, two evaluators reviewing the same dataset may calculate the same metric differently because they interpret field definitions differently. WALKRI resolves that ambiguity before any participant engages, which is the structural precondition for consistent metric measurement across the full PB evaluation cycle.
