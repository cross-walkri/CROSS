# USAID Collaborating, Learning, and Adapting (CLA) Framework

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The USAID CLA Framework, embedded as a requirement in the Agency's Automated Directives System at ADS 201.3.5.19, governs how organizations structure their internal learning cycles, collaborative practices, and adaptive management processes as a condition of program design. CROSS satisfies CLA's structural requirements across all three dimensions: continuous learning, strategic collaboration, and adaptive management. CLA is the first framework in CROSS+WALKRI's coverage that explicitly operationalizes adaptive management as a measurable organizational practice, and CROSS provides the pre-commitment architecture that makes CLA's learning cycles verifiable rather than aspirational.

---

## The CLA Framework's Approach

The CLA Framework operates on two interacting levels: the three substantive dimensions of CLA practice, and the enabling conditions that make those dimensions sustainable. The three dimensions are strategic collaboration (working systematically with stakeholders and partners to leverage resources and share learning), continuous learning (systematically using evidence to inform decisions), and adaptive management (adjusting programs based on what is learned). The enabling conditions are organizational culture and leadership, operational processes that institutionalize learning, and sufficient resources dedicated to learning and adaptation activities.

What distinguishes CLA from evaluation and monitoring frameworks is its scope: CLA does not primarily govern what data an organization collects, but how organizations use evidence to make decisions and adjust their programs. A monitoring framework tells you what to measure. CLA tells you how to structure the organizational behavior that surrounds measurement: who deliberates over evidence, what forums exist for that deliberation, how decisions to adapt are documented, and how learning from one period informs the design of the next. This is an organizational architecture question, not simply a data collection question.

CLA's placement in ADS 201.3.5.19 makes it a Program Cycle requirement, meaning that USAID missions and operating units are expected to integrate CLA practices into program design, implementation, monitoring, and evaluation. Partners working with USAID are increasingly expected to demonstrate CLA-aligned practices, not simply to collect monitoring data but to show structured processes for learning from that data and adapting accordingly. The learning cycle is the unit of accountability: can an organization demonstrate that it examined evidence from a prior period, deliberated over what it meant, and made documented adjustments to its program design or implementation?

The framework also explicitly values collaborative practice as a program design condition, not simply as a project management virtue. Strategic collaboration means that programs identify relevant actors in their operating environment, coordinate with them to avoid duplication and leverage complementary resources, and document the resulting coordination. This has structural implications for how programs describe their coherence with other actors in the same context.

## How CROSS Satisfies the CLA Framework

The CLA "continuous learning" dimension maps to the CROSS continuation gate. Before any program commits to its next round of obligations, CROSS requires a structured documentation exercise: what was learned from the prior period, how that learning was generated, and how it informs the next phase's design. This is not a narrative addendum; it is a gate condition. A program cannot proceed to the next round without producing this documentation. The continuation gate thereby operationalizes CLA's continuous learning requirement, making it a structural condition of program advancement rather than a recommended practice.

The CLA "strategic collaboration" dimension maps to the CROSS coherence disclosure requirement. Programs operating under CROSS must document, at the entry gate, how the program relates to other actors working in the same context, what coordination relationships exist, and how the program avoids duplication or leverages complementarity. This is the structural correlate of CLA's expectation that programs identify and work systematically with relevant stakeholders. The coherence disclosure makes collaboration an explicitly documented design element, not an informal organizational habit.

The CLA "adaptive management" dimension maps to the CROSS causality stance field combined with the continuation gate documentation. The causality stance field requires programs to declare, before implementation, their causal position: what theory of change underlies the program, what evidence supports it, and what would count as evidence that the theory is not holding. The continuation gate then requires, at each cycle end, a comparison between what was expected and what was observed, and documentation of how that comparison informs subsequent design. This is exactly the adaptive management cycle CLA requires: declare a causal position, observe results, compare, document the comparison, and carry learning forward.

| CLA Dimension | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Continuous learning | Continuation gate (prior-period learning documentation required before next round opens) |
| Strategic collaboration | Coherence disclosure at entry gate (coordination with other actors documented) |
| Adaptive management | Causality stance field + continuation gate (causal position declared; comparison to prior evidence documented) |
| Enabling conditions: operational processes | CROSS gate sequence (structured, repeatable process for each program cycle) |
| Enabling conditions: transparency | CROSS pre-commitment requirement (criteria published before applications open) |

CROSS's pre-commitment requirement also serves CLA's underlying transparency aspiration. CLA calls for organizations to make their learning and adaptation processes legible to stakeholders. A funder operating under CROSS publishes its criteria, Theory of Change, and expected outcomes before any application is submitted, making its causal assumptions available for public scrutiny. This creates the conditions under which stakeholders can engage with the program's design, offer feedback, and participate meaningfully in collaborative learning.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI ensures that the intake fields used to collect learning cycle evidence for CLA-aligned programs function as measurement instruments, not open-ended reflection prompts. In a CLA context, this is consequential: if a program's application asks implementing partners to describe their adaptive management practices, a free-text response field does not generate cross-period comparable data. WALKRI requires that such fields specify what counts as a valid response, what evidence form is acceptable, and what threshold of documentation qualifies as demonstrating an adaptive management process.

This means that a WALKRI-compliant intake process for a CLA-aligned program would specify, for each learning-related intake field, the operational definition of the practice being assessed. "Describe your learning cycle" becomes a structured field with defined response criteria: What documentation of prior-period evidence is required? What forum or deliberation process must be described? What documentation of a resulting program adjustment is required? WALKRI converts CLA's qualitative organizational practice requirements into structured data collection, enabling structured cross-period comparison and making the learning cycle evidence auditable.
