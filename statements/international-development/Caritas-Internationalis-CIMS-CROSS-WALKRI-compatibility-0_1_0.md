# Caritas Internationalis Management Standards (CIMS)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Caritas Internationalis Management Standards (CIMS, 2021 revision; caritas.org) constitute a confederation-wide formal accountability standard applied across all 162 member organizations of the Caritas Internationalis confederation. CIMS is the only framework in CROSS+WALKRI's current coverage where a global network of humanitarian and development organizations holds all members to shared governance, financial, programmatic, and safeguarding standards through a formal certification and review cycle, with confederation-level accountability for member compliance. CROSS maps to CIMS across its governance, programs and projects management, and safeguarding sections; WALKRI ensures that intake instruments for CIMS-compliant programs collect evidence in operationally defined, auditable form.

---

## The CIMS Framework's Approach

CIMS is an organizational accountability standard, not a project-level evaluation framework. Its subject is the member organization as an institution: how it governs itself, how it manages financial resources, how it manages its staff, how it designs and manages programs and projects, how it communicates, and how it protects the people it serves. The six CIMS sections cover governance, financial management, human resource management, programs and projects management, communications, and the Safeguarding Standard added in 2020. Each section contains a set of standards against which member organizations are assessed through the Organizational Review Tool, with the confederation providing coordination and accountability across the review cycle.

The confederation review cycle is the distinctive accountability architecture of CIMS. Unlike project-level evaluations or indicator-based monitoring frameworks, CIMS does not assess individual grants or programs in isolation. It assesses whether an organization has the institutional systems, policies, and practices to be accountable to the people it serves and to the confederation. Members are assessed periodically, assessors follow the Reference Guide for Coordinators and Assessors, and findings are reported at the confederation level. This means that a single organization's compliance failures are not purely that organization's private matter; they carry confederation-level accountability implications.

The Safeguarding Standard, added to CIMS in 2020, addresses protection of beneficiaries from harm by staff and representatives of member organizations. It requires member organizations to have formal safeguarding policies, reporting mechanisms, investigation procedures, and accountability structures in place. This is a significant expansion of scope beyond the original CIMS framework, reflecting the humanitarian sector's increased attention to safeguarding failures in global NGOs and confederations.

CIMS's programs and projects management section governs how member organizations design, implement, monitor, evaluate, and close out programs and projects. The section traces the full project lifecycle: inception, implementation, monitoring, evaluation, and closeout. Each phase has associated management requirements, documentation standards, and accountability expectations. Because CIMS operates at the organizational level, these requirements apply to all programs an organization manages, not just those assessed in a specific evaluation cycle.

## How CROSS Satisfies the CIMS Framework

The CIMS Governance section maps directly to CROSS's organizational identity fields and conflict of interest provisions. CROSS requires programs to document legal name, jurisdiction, registration, governance structure, and disbursement authority at the entry gate. These fields satisfy CIMS's governance documentation requirements: a program operating under CROSS produces, as a condition of opening, the organizational identity documentation that CIMS requires member organizations to maintain and make available for review. CROSS Part VII's conflict of interest provisions, which require disclosure and management of board-level and staff-level conflicts, satisfy CIMS's governance requirements for board independence and conflict management.

The CIMS Programs and Projects Management section maps to the CROSS four-gate sequence. The CIMS lifecycle phases correspond to CROSS gates in the following sequence: inception corresponds to the CROSS entry gate (program design and specification established before implementation begins); implementation corresponds to the CROSS progress gate (ongoing documentation of implementation status against specified outcomes); monitoring corresponds to the CROSS progress gate documentation requirements (structured evidence of progress against indicators); evaluation corresponds to the CROSS completion gate (documented assessment of outcomes against pre-specified targets); and closeout corresponds to the CROSS continuation gate (structured documentation of learning and decisions about whether and how to proceed). This is not a loose analogy. CIMS requires that each lifecycle phase produce documented outputs, and CROSS requires that each gate produce specific records held in the Attestation Corpus, providing the documentation infrastructure CIMS requires.

The CIMS Safeguarding Standard maps to CROSS's conflict of interest provisions in Part VII and to WALKRI's evidence form independence requirement. CROSS Part VII requires that evidence sources be independent from the populations assessed: a program cannot rely exclusively on self-reported data from staff or beneficiaries where independence is a safeguarding concern. The WALKRI evidence form independence requirement operationalizes this at the intake level: intake fields cannot use evidence sources that create structural conflicts between the assessor and the assessed.

The CIMS financial management requirements map to CROSS's concurrent funding disclosure and Attestation Corpus financial record persistence. CROSS requires that programs disclose all concurrent funding sources and the terms under which those sources operate, and that these disclosures be retained in the Attestation Corpus. CIMS requires member organizations to maintain transparent financial management systems and records. The Attestation Corpus provides the persistent financial record infrastructure that CIMS audits require.

| CIMS Section | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Governance: organizational identity | CROSS entry gate organizational identity fields (legal name, jurisdiction, registration) |
| Governance: conflict of interest | CROSS Part VII conflict of interest provisions |
| Financial management | CROSS concurrent funding disclosure + Attestation Corpus financial records |
| Programs and projects: inception | CROSS entry gate (program design and specification) |
| Programs and projects: implementation/monitoring | CROSS progress gate sequence |
| Programs and projects: evaluation | CROSS completion gate |
| Programs and projects: closeout | CROSS continuation gate |
| Safeguarding Standard | CROSS Part VII independence provisions + WALKRI evidence form independence requirement |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI addresses a gap that CIMS creates at the intake level: when member organizations design intake processes for their programs and projects, CIMS's management standards require that those processes produce data adequate for the monitoring, evaluation, and accountability requirements of the relevant CIMS section. WALKRI ensures that intake fields are measurement instruments, not open-ended documentation prompts, which is the practical difference between an intake process that will produce CIMS-auditable evidence and one that will not.

In a safeguarding context, this is especially consequential. WALKRI-compliant intake fields for safeguarding-related program data must specify what counts as a valid safeguarding disclosure, what evidence form is required, what the independence threshold is for the evidence source, and what verification pathway applies. This prevents the common failure mode in which safeguarding intake instruments collect narratives that cannot be compared, assessed for compliance thresholds, or submitted as structured evidence to confederation-level reviewers. WALKRI converts CIMS's organizational accountability requirements into structured data collection specifications at the program design stage.
