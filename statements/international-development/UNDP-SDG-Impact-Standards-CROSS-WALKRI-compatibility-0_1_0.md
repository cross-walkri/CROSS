# UN SDG Impact Standards for Financing Sustainable Development (IS-FSD)

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The OECD-UNDP Impact Standards for Financing Sustainable Development (IS-FSD), published jointly in 2021, govern how organizations structure their strategies, management processes, and governance to align with the Sustainable Development Goals. IS-FSD is a decision-making standard applied at the organizational portfolio level, not a performance-reporting standard applied at the individual program level. It asks whether organizations are structured to make SDG-aligned decisions, not whether individual programs achieved specific metrics. CROSS satisfies all four IS-FSD dimensions through its pre-commitment architecture, gate sequence, conflict of interest provisions, and Attestation Corpus; WALKRI ensures that intake fields for IS-FSD-aligned programs establish SDG-aligned measurement instruments before any application is submitted.

---

## The IS-FSD's Approach

The IS-FSD represents a conceptually distinct category of accountability standard from all others in CROSS+WALKRI's current coverage. Most evaluation and accountability frameworks operate at the program or project level: they define what programs must measure, how they must report, and what evidence standards apply to individual grants. IS-FSD operates at the organizational portfolio level. It asks whether an organization, as a whole, is structured to integrate SDG alignment into its strategy, management, transparency, and governance processes. Individual program performance is evidence that feeds into IS-FSD assessment; it is not the primary subject of the standard.

This distinction matters because IS-FSD compliance requires organizational architecture, not just program design. An organization can have excellent program-level measurement and reporting while having no coherent organizational strategy for SDG alignment, no management processes that systematically use evidence to adapt that strategy, no transparency about how it allocates resources across SDG priorities, and no governance structures ensuring accountability for SDG-aligned decision-making. IS-FSD addresses all four of these dimensions: Strategy, Management, Transparency, and Governance.

The IS-FSD was developed jointly by the OECD and UNDP to provide a framework for how private finance actors, development finance institutions, and public funders can credibly align their portfolios with the SDGs. Three versions of the standard exist: for enterprises, for bond issuers, and for financing sustainable development specifically. The IS-FSD version addresses the accountability architecture of organizations that deploy financing for development purposes, including grant-making organizations, blended finance vehicles, and development finance institutions. The standard operates within the broader SDG Impact ecosystem at UNDP, which includes the SDG Impact Seal and a growing assurance infrastructure.

Critically, IS-FSD does not prescribe which SDGs an organization must prioritize, or what specific program models it must use. It prescribes how organizations should structure the processes by which they make those choices. This makes IS-FSD a process standard at the organizational level, analogous to the way ISO quality management standards govern process without prescribing product specifications.

## How CROSS Satisfies the IS-FSD

The IS-FSD Strategy dimension requires that organizations articulate a clear theory of how their strategy contributes to the SDGs, set objectives aligned with SDG targets, and allocate resources coherently with those objectives. The CROSS Theory of Change goal declaration satisfies this at the program level: programs must articulate, before any application opens, the SDG-relevant goal to which the program contributes, the intermediate outcomes expected to lead to that goal, and the outputs that will produce those outcomes. The CROSS coherence disclosure satisfies the portfolio coherence requirement: programs must document how they relate to other actors and programs operating in the same SDG context. Together, these two CROSS mechanisms make the Strategy dimension verifiable at the program level.

The IS-FSD Management dimension requires that organizations use evidence to make decisions, have processes for monitoring performance against SDG-aligned objectives, and adapt their strategies and operations based on what they learn. The CROSS gate sequence satisfies all three requirements: the gate architecture is a structured, evidence-based decision process applied at each program cycle. Gate decisions are not discretionary; they require specified evidence at each transition point. The continuation gate specifically requires that prior-period evidence be examined and that its implications for next-period design be documented. This is the adaptive management process IS-FSD requires, made into a structural condition of program continuation.

The IS-FSD Transparency dimension requires that organizations publicly disclose how they integrate the SDGs into their strategy and operations, using credible and verifiable evidence. The CROSS pre-commitment requirement satisfies the credibility dimension: criteria, Theory of Change, and expected outcomes are published before any application is submitted, making them independently verifiable before results are claimed. The CROSS Attestation Corpus satisfies the persistence and verifiability requirements: gate records are held as a persistent, publicly queryable record of design commitments and gate determinations, enabling any stakeholder to verify the organization's stated approach against its actual decisions.

The IS-FSD Governance dimension requires that organizations have governance structures ensuring accountability for SDG-aligned decision-making, including management of conflicts of interest that could undermine SDG alignment. CROSS Part VII conflict of interest provisions satisfy this directly: programs must document governance-level conflicts and demonstrate that disbursement decisions are insulated from them.

| IS-FSD Dimension | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Strategy: SDG-aligned theory of change | CROSS Theory of Change goal declaration at entry gate |
| Strategy: portfolio coherence | CROSS coherence disclosure at entry gate |
| Management: evidence-based decision process | CROSS gate sequence (gate decisions require specified evidence at each transition) |
| Management: adaptive management | CROSS continuation gate (prior-period evidence examined; implications documented) |
| Transparency: credible public disclosure | CROSS pre-commitment requirement (criteria published before applications open) |
| Transparency: persistent verifiable records | CROSS Attestation Corpus (gate records publicly queryable) |
| Governance: conflict of interest management | CROSS Part VII conflict of interest provisions |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI ensures that intake fields for IS-FSD-aligned programs establish SDG target documentation, context baselines, and contribution evidence as measurement instruments before any application is submitted. In an IS-FSD context, this means that intake fields asking applicants to document their contribution to specific SDG targets must specify what a valid contribution claim requires: which SDG target is cited, what baseline condition is documented, what measurement methodology is specified, and what evidence pathway will be used to assess contribution. WALKRI prevents the common failure mode in which IS-FSD-aligned intake processes collect SDG citations without establishing the evidential basis for assessing whether those citations are credible.

This is consequential for IS-FSD's Transparency dimension in particular. If intake instruments do not require operationally defined contribution claims, the organization's transparency disclosures rest on assertions that cannot be independently verified against a defined evidential standard. WALKRI converts IS-FSD's portfolio-level transparency requirements into program-level intake design specifications, ensuring that the evidence required for IS-FSD Transparency reporting is built into the data collection architecture from the outset.
