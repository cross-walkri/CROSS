# Head Start and Early Head Start Program Performance Standards

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Head Start and Early Head Start Program Performance Standards (45 CFR Part 1302), administered by the Office of Head Start within the Administration for Children and Families (ACF), govern approximately $10 billion in annual federal funding serving approximately one million children per program year. The standards establish a domain-structured child outcome measurement architecture anchored to the Head Start Early Learning Outcomes Framework (ELOF) that is substantively more specific than the general 2 CFR 200 uniform guidance applicable to most federal grants. CROSS+WALKRI satisfies the Part 1302 evaluation architecture structurally: the ELOF's five developmental domains constitute a pre-specified indicator framework that programs must align to before any child is enrolled, which is the entry specification gate applied to child outcomes; WALKRI's operational definition and evidence form requirements, applied to child assessment instruments before any intake field is published, produce the instrument-level specification that Part 1302 requires but does not itself supply.

---

## The Head Start Program Performance Standards Evaluation Framework

The Head Start Program Performance Standards establish evaluation requirements that differ from general federal grants guidance in four ways that are structurally significant for CROSS+WALKRI compatibility analysis.

**School readiness goal alignment to the ELOF:** 45 CFR Part 1302, Subpart B requires each Head Start program to establish school readiness goals aligned to the Head Start Early Learning Outcomes Framework across its five developmental domains: language and literacy development, cognition and general knowledge, approaches to learning, physical well-being and motor development, and social and emotional development. These goals must be established before the program year opens, not derived post-hoc from observed child outcomes. This pre-enrollment specification requirement, applied to domain-specific child outcomes, is the regulatory form of CROSS's entry specification gate operating at the child outcome level rather than the program output level.

**Tri-annual child assessment aggregation:** 45 CFR 1302.33 requires programs to aggregate and analyze child-level assessment data at minimum three times per program year. This structured interim progress verification cycle corresponds to CROSS's completion gate sequence: programs do not wait until program end to review evidence against their stated indicators. The three-point aggregation also creates the longitudinal comparison baseline that CROSS's Fields 3 and 4 (baseline and target) require.

**Annual self-assessment using aggregated data:** 45 CFR 1302.102 requires each funded program to conduct an annual self-assessment using aggregated child and program performance data, covering health, family services, staff qualifications, and program environment, in addition to child developmental outcomes. This structured annual review corresponds to CROSS's attestation gate: evidence of what the program produced is reviewed against what it stated it would produce.

**Mandatory public annual program report:** Programs must publicly disseminate an annual program report. This public disclosure requirement produces an independently accessible attestation corpus, which is the function CROSS's attestation gate is designed to create.

**Program Information Report (PIR):** All Head Start grantees submit the PIR annually through the Head Start Enterprise System (HSES), reporting child demographics, health status, disability status, staff qualifications, family services, and program characteristics. The PIR is not a self-designed reporting instrument; it is a nationally standardized data collection that specifies exactly what fields each program must populate and what units of measurement apply. This corresponds to a nationally pre-specified indicator set with mandatory disaggregation by age, disability status, race, and ethnicity.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Part 1302 Framework

CROSS addresses the Part 1302 evaluation requirements at the structural level.

**Entry specification gate and school readiness goals:** The ELOF's five developmental domains, and the requirement that programs establish school readiness goals aligned to those domains before enrollment begins, constitute the institutional form of CROSS's entry specification gate applied to child outcome indicators. Programs must specify which ELOF-aligned outcomes they are working toward, for which children, before any child is served. CROSS's eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1-5: name, definition, unit, baseline, target) applied to ELOF-domain school readiness goals produces entries that satisfy Part 1302's goal specification requirement and provide the measurement precision the regulation requires but does not itself mandate.

**Tri-annual aggregation and CROSS progress verification:** The mandatory tri-annual data aggregation cycle corresponds to CROSS's completion gate structure, which requires evidence review at defined intervals rather than only at program end. Programs operating under CROSS and Part 1302 simultaneously use the three required aggregation points as natural completion gate checkpoints, comparing aggregated child outcome data against the school readiness goals declared at program year start.

**PIR and disaggregation ratchet:** The PIR's mandatory demographic fields (race, ethnicity, age, disability status) correspond to CROSS's disaggregation ratchet requirement: once demographic disaggregation is established at program enrollment, it cannot be removed in later reporting periods. Programs reporting through the PIR satisfy CROSS's disaggregation ratchet as a structural consequence of HSES system requirements, since the PIR's field definitions are fixed nationally.

**Causality stance and school readiness attribution:** Part 1302's school readiness framework requires programs to connect their services to child developmental outcomes across five ELOF domains. CROSS's causality stance field (Field 10) documents whether the program claims attribution or contribution for each domain-level outcome, and what comparison group informs that claim. For Head Start programs, the appropriate causality stance is typically contribution with a program-to-comparison-community reference: Head Start's program evaluation literature includes large-scale impact studies that can serve as the comparison basis CROSS's Field 9 (comparison group) requires.

| Part 1302 Requirement | CROSS Provision |
|:--|:--|
| ELOF school readiness goal alignment pre-enrollment | Entry specification gate (indicators pre-specified before any child enrolled) |
| Five ELOF developmental domain coverage | Theory of Change hierarchy (activities, outputs, outcomes, goals declared at entry gate) |
| Tri-annual child assessment aggregation | Completion gate checkpoints (evidence reviewed at defined intervals) |
| Annual self-assessment using aggregated data | Attestation gate (structured evidence review against pre-specified indicators) |
| Mandatory public annual program report | Attestation corpus (publicly accessible evidence of program results) |
| PIR mandatory demographic fields | Disaggregation ratchet (demographic categories maintained across all reporting periods) |
| National PIR indicator standardization | Eleven-field indicator specification applied to nationally standardized indicator set |
| Child-level health and disability status | Population scope declaration (who is served and what subgroup distinctions apply) |
| Longitudinal three-point child outcome data | Fields 3-4 (baseline and target) + Fields 7-9 (timing, data source, collection method) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

The Part 1302 framework specifies what must be measured across the five ELOF domains but does not specify, at the intake field level, how assessment instruments must define proficiency, what score on a given domain measure constitutes the evidence threshold, or what documentary form the measurement must take. This specification gap is precisely where WALKRI's pre-publication requirements operate.

WALKRI's five pre-publication requirements applied to child assessment intake fields resolve the most common source of incomparable data across Head Start programs: two programs may both report measuring "language and literacy development" using ELOF-aligned goals while using different instruments, different scoring conventions, and different thresholds for what counts as "on track." WALKRI's operational definition requirement forces each program to specify, before any intake field is published, what the outcome means in measurable terms. The evidence form requirement specifies what assessment instrument is used, how it is scored, and what score constitutes the evidence threshold. The compliance threshold requirement specifies what level of outcome at what point in the program year constitutes satisfactory progress against the declared school readiness goal.

For the PIR's demographic fields, WALKRI's pre-publication audit confirms that the field definitions from the HSES national specification are faithfully reproduced in the program's own intake instruments, so that what the program collects locally matches what it reports through HSES. The response form requirement, applied to PIR-specified demographic fields before any family completes enrollment paperwork, ensures that the options presented to families map correctly to the PIR's national category definitions. Programs that complete this audit before the program year opens eliminate the reconciliation burden that otherwise arises when locally collected categories do not align with HSES submission requirements.
