# Accountable Now Global Standard for CSO Accountability

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Global Standard for CSO Accountability, co-hosted by Accountable Now (accountablenow.org) and the CSO Standard coalition (csostandard.org), is the primary international accountability framework for civil society organizations as grant recipients. Launched in December 2017 with twelve civil society networks, it establishes 12 Commitments across three clusters: what CSOs aim to achieve, how they approach change, and how they shift internal practices. CROSS+WALKRI produces the evidentiary infrastructure that conformant reporting against these 12 Commitments requires, and the CROSS Attestation Corpus provides the persistent, publicly accessible record that the framework's independent review requirement demands.

---

## The Global Standard for CSO Accountability

Accountable Now co-hosts the Global Standard for CSO Accountability alongside the CSO Standard initiative, which launched in December 2017 with twelve civil society networks worldwide. The standard succeeded the 2006 INGO Accountability Charter and substantially expanded both its scope and its structural rigor. Where the Charter addressed primarily large international NGOs, the Global Standard is designed to apply across the civil society sector, including smaller grantee organizations, coalitions, and networks.

The 12 Commitments are organized in three clusters. The first cluster addresses what CSOs aim to achieve: mission clarity, human rights alignment, and commitment to stated social impact goals. The second cluster addresses how organizations approach change: meaningful stakeholder participation in programming and learning, diversity and inclusion practices, and environmental responsibility. The third cluster addresses how organizations shift their own internal practices: governance and board accountability, transparency through public reporting, conflict of interest management, and ethical fundraising practices.

Member organizations report annually against all 12 Commitments through an Independent Review Panel. Reports are published publicly. This architecture creates a two-level accountability structure: organizations commit to the standard, report against it annually, and have those reports reviewed by an independent body whose determinations are also publicly available. The combination of self-reporting, independent review, and public disclosure is the core accountability mechanism.

For cohort 1 of the grants ecosystem (civil society organizations as grant recipients) and cohort 3 (program operators managing civil society grantee programs), alignment with the Global Standard is both a credibility signal to funders and an internal governance discipline. The independent review requirement in particular creates a structural demand for persistent, verifiable records that can support external assessment.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Global Standard

CROSS addresses the Global Standard's structural requirements at three levels: documentation (what records exist), transparency (who can access them), and governance (what controls are in place). The table below maps each relevant Commitment to the CROSS mechanism that produces or enforces the corresponding requirement.

| Global Standard Commitment | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Transparency (public annual reporting against all 12 Commitments) | CROSS Attestation Corpus: persistent, publicly accessible record of all gate determinations and organizational declarations, queryable by any interested party. The Attestation Corpus is the reporting substrate that annual transparency commitments require. |
| Participation (meaningful stakeholder involvement in programming and learning) | CROSS population scope declaration + evidence source specification: documents who is affected by the program and from whom evidence of effect is drawn. This creates a verifiable record of whether stakeholder populations were defined and consulted. |
| Independent review requirement | CROSS independent attestation pathway: the highest-evidence-strength option in CROSS's evidence taxonomy, enabling third-party verification of gate determinations by a party independent of the grantee. |
| Governance (board accountability, conflict of interest) | CROSS Part VII conflict of interest provisions + organizational identity declaration: legal structure, governance model, and disbursement authority fields document the organizational governance infrastructure that the Governance Commitment requires. |
| Ethical fundraising (accurate representation of programs and outcomes) | WALKRI evidence form requirement: specifies an independent evidence access path, separating program outcome evidence from beneficiary self-report. This structural separation implements the accuracy requirement that ethical fundraising demands. |
| Mission clarity | CROSS Theory of Change declaration: required at entry gate, documents the causal logic linking program activities to stated outcomes. |

The transparency mapping deserves particular attention. The Global Standard's transparency commitment requires that organizations publish annual reports against all 12 Commitments, making their accountability record accessible to funders, peers, and the public. CROSS's Attestation Corpus is designed precisely for this use: it is a persistent, publicly accessible record that accumulates over the lifetime of a program, with each gate determination adding a new layer of documented performance. An organization with a CROSS-conformant program history has the evidentiary basis for transparent annual reporting without needing to reconstruct it retrospectively.

The participation mapping addresses a common gap in CSO accountability reporting. Organizations frequently assert meaningful stakeholder participation without being able to document who the relevant stakeholders are, how they are defined, and from whom evidence of engagement is drawn. CROSS's population scope declaration requires this documentation as a condition of entry gate approval. The record exists before the claim is made, rather than being assembled to support a claim after the fact.

The governance mapping connects CROSS's organizational identity fields to the governance cluster. The Governance Commitment requires that boards exercise meaningful oversight, including over conflict of interest. CROSS Part VII provides the structural provisions; the disbursement authority field documents whether funds are controlled by an individual, an organization, or a governance mechanism, which is precisely the information an independent reviewer would need to assess whether board-level control is genuine.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements ensure that the annual accountability reports produced by conformant programs are based on precisely defined measurement instruments rather than post-hoc narrative reconstruction. When an organization reports annually against the Global Standard's program impact commitments, the credibility of that report depends on whether the underlying data was collected with sufficient precision to support the claims being made.

A WALKRI-conformant program defines its measurement terms, units, baselines, evidence access paths, and population scope before any applicant or beneficiary provides data. The annual report then draws on data that was specified in advance, collected consistently, and accessible to an independent reviewer through the documented evidence access path. This is the evidentiary standard that the Independent Review Panel process implicitly requires: reports that can be checked, not merely asserted.

The ethical fundraising commitment also receives direct support from WALKRI. The evidence form requirement separates program outcome evidence from beneficiary self-report, ensuring that the outcome data an organization cites in fundraising and reporting is not circular. This structural separation is the difference between "beneficiaries report that they benefited" (self-report) and "independent evidence from a specified source documents that the specified indicator changed by the specified amount in the specified population" (WALKRI-conformant evidence). The distinction matters for the Independent Review Panel and for any funder assessing whether a CSO's reporting meets the accuracy standard that the Global Standard requires.
