# Grant Professionals Certification (GPC) Competency Standards

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Grant Professionals Certification (GPC), issued by the Grant Professionals Certification Institute (GPCI) and accredited by the National Commission for Certifying Agencies (NCCA), is the recognized professional credential for grant practitioners in the United States. CROSS+WALKRI satisfies the structural requirements implied by GPC's nine competency domains and explicitly implements the ethics standards encoded in the GPA Code of Ethics. Grantors deploying CROSS+WALKRI-conformant programs create the evidentiary infrastructure that a GPC-credentialed grant program operator would be expected to produce and maintain.

---

## The GPC Competency Framework

The GPC credential, administered by GPCI and accredited by the NCCA, covers nine competency domains assessed on a weighted basis. The three highest-weighted domains are: crafting and submitting grant applications (23%), researching and matching funding sources (18%), and effective program and project design (17%). Post-award grant management accounts for 10% of assessed competency, as do nationally recognized ethical practices. Organizational development for grant-seeking accounts for 9%, and relationship cultivation between funders and grantees for 7%.

The NCCA accreditation gives GPC formal standing comparable to a published professional standard rather than merely a voluntary credential. Organizations that employ GPC-credentialed staff or require GPC alignment signal to funders that grant administration meets a documented level of professional rigor. For cohort 3 of the grants ecosystem (grant program operators), GPC competency domains effectively describe what a well-run grant program does at each stage of the grant lifecycle.

The GPA Code of Ethics, published by the Grant Professionals Association, establishes binding ethics requirements for credential holders. The prohibition on contingency and percentage-fee arrangements removes a structural incentive for grant writers to inflate expected outcomes in proportion to grant size. The confidentiality and accuracy requirements mandate that information submitted in applications and reports reflects accurate data rather than strategically favorable characterizations.

Together, the competency domains and ethics requirements describe a practitioner standard whose structural demands align closely with what CROSS and WALKRI require of conformant programs at the system level.

## How CROSS Satisfies the GPC Framework

CROSS addresses GPC competency domains structurally rather than aspirationally. The table below maps each relevant domain to the CROSS mechanism that produces or enforces the corresponding requirement.

| GPC Competency Domain | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| Program and project design (17%) | Theory of Change declaration + eleven-field indicator specification (Fields 1-11). The structured design outputs a GPC practitioner would produce in the design phase are required for CROSS entry gate. |
| Crafting and submitting applications (23%) | Upstream of CROSS scope, but WALKRI directly addresses this domain (see below). CROSS post-award requirements ensure application commitments are measurable. |
| Post-award grant management (10%) | CROSS progress verification gate and completion gate. The gate records produced at each structured review interval are the performance reports a GPC-credentialed operator generates. |
| Relationship cultivation (7%) | CROSS coherence disclosure and gate determination records create structured documentation of funder-grantee communication at each decision point throughout the grant lifecycle. |
| Organizational development for grant-seeking (9%) | CROSS organizational identity declaration (legal structure, disbursement authority, jurisdiction) documents the organizational infrastructure relevant to grant-seeking capacity. |
| Nationally recognized ethical practices (10%) | CROSS Part VII conflict of interest provisions + WALKRI evidence form requirement (separating evidence from beneficiary self-report implements accuracy and integrity standards structurally rather than relying on individual practitioner compliance). |

The post-award grant management mapping deserves elaboration. A GPC-credentialed program operator is expected to produce structured performance reports at defined intervals during the grant period, documenting progress against agreed indicators. CROSS progress verification gate records are precisely those reports: they specify which indicators are being assessed, what evidence was reviewed, what determination was reached, and when. The gate record format is not an approximation of what a performance report should contain; it is a structured implementation of the GPC post-award competency.

The ethics domain mapping is similarly direct. The GPA Code of Ethics prohibits misrepresentation and requires accuracy in reporting. CROSS Part VII addresses conflicts of interest at the organizational level. The structural separation of evidence from beneficiary self-report, required by WALKRI's evidence form specification, implements the accuracy requirement in the data collection phase rather than relying on retrospective review.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication audit requirement addresses the crafting and submitting grant applications domain, the highest-weighted GPC competency, from the opposite direction. Experienced grant writers have an informational advantage when grant program forms are loosely specified: ambiguous prompts can be answered in ways that maximize persuasive effect without necessarily maximizing accuracy. WALKRI's five field-level requirements (defined term, unit of measurement, baseline, evidence access path, and population scope) close this asymmetry before any applicant responds.

A WALKRI-conformant intake form is equally legible to a subject matter expert with no grant writing experience and to a GPC-credentialed professional. The information elicited is the same; the form cannot be navigated differently depending on how sophisticated the applicant is about grant writing conventions. This directly implements the GPA ethics requirement that application submissions reflect accurate data, by ensuring the form itself specifies what accurate data looks like at each field.
