# AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards (2011) Compatibility

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

CROSS+WALKRI satisfies the five property groups of the Joint Committee on Standards for Educational Evaluation Program Evaluation Standards through structural provisions that address utility, feasibility, propriety, accuracy, and evaluation accountability as architectural consequences of conformance. The Accuracy standards, which require measurement instruments to be specified with sufficient precision to allow replication, are satisfied in a particularly direct way: WALKRI's pre-publication audit requirement enforces instrument-level specification before any applicant encounters a data collection form.

The JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards (third edition, 2011) are the formal published standards of the global evaluation profession, endorsed by the American Evaluation Association, the Canadian Evaluation Society, and twenty-plus additional professional societies. Thirty individual standards are organized under the five properties: Utility (U1-U8), Feasibility (F1-F4), Propriety (P1-P7), Accuracy (A1-A8), and Evaluation Accountability (E1-E3). Adoption of CROSS+WALKRI by a grant program constitutes adoption of a conformant documentation and measurement architecture that addresses each property group by design.

## The AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards' Approach

The JCSEE standards treat evaluation as a disciplined inquiry process requiring: that evaluators be clear about purpose and users before data collection begins; that evaluation designs be practical given real-world resource and political constraints; that evaluation processes respect the rights and dignity of participants; that claims be grounded in sufficient, reliable, and valid evidence; and that evaluators be accountable for their own conduct and findings.

The Utility standards (U1-U8) are concerned with whether evaluation processes and findings actually serve the information needs of intended users. They require that evaluators identify stakeholders, clarify purposes, communicate findings in ways that are understandable and timely, and promote the use of findings in program decisions. These are not process requirements for their own sake; they are conditions for an evaluation to function as a decision-support instrument rather than a compliance exercise.

The Feasibility standards (F1-F4) require that evaluation designs be realistic, politically viable, efficient, and cost-effective. Evidence requirements must be calibrated to what is actually achievable within the program's operating context. An evaluation design that requires evidence no program can realistically produce satisfies no standard in the JCSEE framework; it simply creates a gap between formal obligation and actual practice.

The Propriety standards (P1-P7) address the ethical foundations of evaluation. They require that evaluators manage conflicts of interest, protect participant rights, ensure that evaluation processes are equitable, and produce findings that are complete rather than selectively favorable. Particularly relevant to grant program evaluation is P3, which concerns the clarity and fairness of evaluation agreements, and P7, which addresses conflicts of interest directly.

The Accuracy standards (A1-A8) are the most technically demanding cluster. They require that evaluation designs be grounded in defensible information-gathering methods, that measurement procedures be described with sufficient precision to allow replication, and that claims be warranted by the evidence produced. A4 (Explicit Program and Context Descriptions) requires that the program being evaluated be described with enough specificity that its boundaries and theory of operation are clear. A5 (Information Management) requires systematic data handling. A6 (Sound Designs and Analyses) and A7 (Explicit Evaluation Reasoning) require that inferences from evidence to claims follow transparent logical pathways.

The Evaluation Accountability standards (E1-E3) require that evaluators document their own evaluation processes, findings, and the basis for judgments in ways that allow those processes to be reviewed, questioned, and improved. They create an obligation for evaluators to be transparent about what they did, what they found, and how they reached their conclusions.

## How CROSS Satisfies the AEA/JCSEE Program Evaluation Standards

| JCSEE Property | CROSS Provision | Assessment |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| Utility (U1-U8) | Pre-commitment requirement: evaluation criteria published before submissions arrive; findings structured for funder decision gates | Full satisfaction. CROSS's gate architecture ensures that evaluation criteria are specified in advance and that gate outputs directly inform program decisions (continuation, modification, termination). The pre-commitment requirement satisfies U3 (Negotiated Purposes) and U5 (Relevant Information). |
| Feasibility (F1-F4) | Proportionality principle: evidence requirements scaled to program size and obligation mode (developmental vs. summative) | Full satisfaction. CROSS's proportionality principle satisfies F1 (Practical Procedures) and F3 (Contextual Viability) by requiring that evidence obligations be calibrated to what is achievable in the program's operating context. Small programs are not required to produce the same evidence load as large ones. |
| Propriety (P1-P7, especially P7) | Part VII conflict of interest provisions: conditions under which a party may serve as reviewer or attestor | Full satisfaction of P7 (Conflicts of Interest). CROSS Part VII specifies the structural conditions under which a reviewer or attestor is disqualified, required to disclose, or permitted to participate with disclosure. This is a structural implementation of conflict of interest management rather than a reliance on individual evaluator judgment. |
| Accuracy (A1-A8) | WALKRI five pre-publication field requirements: criterion intent, operational definition, response form, evidence form, compliance threshold | Full satisfaction of A2 (Valid Information), A3 (Reliable Information), A4 (Explicit Program and Context Descriptions), A6 (Sound Designs and Analyses). See WALKRI section below. |
| Evaluation Accountability (E1-E3) | CROSS Attestation Corpus: verifiable records of all prior gate determinations, documentation trail | Full satisfaction. CROSS's Attestation Corpus creates a time-stamped, verifiable record of all gate determinations, the evidence submitted, and the basis for each decision. This is the structural implementation of E1 (Evaluation Documentation), E2 (Internal Metaevaluation), and E3 (External Metaevaluation). |

**Result:** CROSS satisfies all five JCSEE property groups as structural consequences of conformance. No supplementary evaluation architecture is required to achieve alignment.

The Accuracy mapping deserves elaboration. JCSEE's Accuracy standards require measurement procedures specified with sufficient precision to allow replication. This is precisely what WALKRI's five pre-publication field requirements produce. The criterion intent field specifies what the measurement is trying to detect. The operational definition field specifies how the criterion is bounded. The response form field specifies the format in which an applicant or grantee is expected to provide information. The evidence form field specifies what artifact constitutes acceptable evidence. The compliance threshold field specifies the minimum standard that separates compliance from non-compliance. These five fields, taken together, constitute a replication-ready measurement instrument specification. An independent evaluator reading a WALKRI-conformant field specification can reconstruct the measurement procedure without access to the original designer's intent.

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI's pre-publication audit is the structural implementation of the JCSEE Accuracy standards applied at the instrument design stage rather than retrospectively during review. Conventional evaluation practice frequently produces Accuracy standard violations through poorly specified data collection instruments: questions that are ambiguous, response formats that accept incommensurable answer types, or evidence requirements that are stated in terms too general to produce consistent judgments across reviewers. By requiring that each intake field pass a five-element specification check before it is published to applicants, WALKRI eliminates this class of Accuracy violation at source.

The JCSEE framework treats Accuracy as a property of the overall evaluation process; WALKRI treats it as a property of each measurement instrument within that process. These are complementary scopes. A program that uses WALKRI-conformant intake fields and produces a CROSS-conformant gate determination record satisfies the JCSEE Accuracy standards both at the instrument level and at the process level. Programs reporting to AEA institutional members, foundation program officers trained in AEA competencies, or funders who reference the JCSEE standards in their evaluation policies do not need to produce supplementary documentation to demonstrate Accuracy standard compliance; WALKRI conformance is sufficient.
