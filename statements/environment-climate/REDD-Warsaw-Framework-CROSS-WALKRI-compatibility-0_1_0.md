# REDD+ Warsaw Framework for Forest Carbon MRV

## CROSS+WALKRI Compatibility Statement v0.1.0

---

## Summary

The Warsaw Framework for REDD+ (established at COP 19, 2013; redd.unfccc.int) sets out the sovereign-level measurement, reporting, and verification requirements for programs reducing emissions from deforestation and forest degradation in developing countries. Its four distinctive primitives (a mandatory counterfactual baseline, a two-step technical assessment process, national forest monitoring systems as an institutional precondition, and results submitted through biennial transparency reports) correspond to specific CROSS mechanisms at each stage of the program cycle. Grant-funded forestry, land-use, and conservation programs operating within national REDD+ frameworks must meet these sovereign-level requirements, and CROSS provides the structural architecture to satisfy them. WALKRI ensures that intake instruments for REDD+-linked programs establish forest type, monitoring methodology, reference period, and evidence access path before any project registers.

---

## The Warsaw Framework's Approach

REDD+ is the UNFCCC mechanism for incentivizing developing countries to reduce emissions from deforestation and forest degradation, conserve and enhance forest carbon stocks, and sustainably manage forests. The Warsaw Framework (COP 19, 2013) established the foundational rules governing how countries measure, report, and verify REDD+ results, and how results-based payments are structured and disbursed. The framework operates at the sovereign level: individual projects and programs exist within national REDD+ systems, and the measurement and verification requirements flow from sovereign-level commitments to project-level implementation, not the other way around.

The first distinctive primitive of the Warsaw Framework is the Forest Reference Emission Level or Forest Reference Level (FREL/FRL). This is a mandatory counterfactual baseline established at the sovereign level: before any REDD+ results can be claimed, the country must submit an FREL/FRL to the UNFCCC documenting what the historical emissions trajectory from deforestation and degradation has been, and what the expected trajectory would be in the absence of REDD+ interventions. Measured reductions are assessed against this counterfactual, not against an arbitrary target. A country cannot claim credit for reduced deforestation unless it has first documented what deforestation would have occurred without the intervention.

The second distinctive primitive is the two-step technical assessment process. Countries submit their REDD+ results to the UNFCCC in a Results Report, which is then subject to a separate technical review by a team of UNFCCC-accredited experts. Submission and review are distinct steps, each with its own documentation and procedural requirements. The two-step structure ensures that results claims are separated from the assessment of those claims, preventing the conflict of interest that would arise if self-reported results were automatically accepted.

The third distinctive primitive is the national forest monitoring system as an institutional precondition. Countries cannot register REDD+ results without demonstrating that they have a functioning national forest monitoring system capable of producing the data required. This is a capacity precondition, not simply a documentation requirement: the monitoring infrastructure must exist before results can be measured and reported. The fourth primitive is the submission pathway: REDD+ results are submitted as technical annexes to a country's Biennial Transparency Report under the UNFCCC's Enhanced Transparency Framework, embedding REDD+ reporting in the sovereign climate accountability architecture.

The Green Climate Fund adopted REDD+ results-based payments as a permanent financing window in October 2024, making this framework newly relevant for grant-funded conservation programs seeking to access GCF resources. Programs operating within national REDD+ frameworks that seek GCF results-based payments must satisfy the Warsaw Framework's MRV requirements at every stage.

## How CROSS Satisfies the Warsaw Framework

The FREL/FRL mandatory counterfactual baseline corresponds directly to the CROSS counterfactual baseline requirement. Under CROSS, programs must declare a pre-intervention baseline before any implementation begins, against which measured changes will be assessed. The FREL/FRL is the sovereign-scale implementation of exactly this requirement: deforestation emissions are measured against the documented counterfactual trajectory, not against an arbitrary reduction target. A CROSS-compliant program operating within a national REDD+ framework would establish the relevant portion of the national FREL/FRL as its counterfactual baseline at the entry gate, making the relationship between the program's measurement approach and the sovereign-level reference transparent before any implementation begins.

The two-step technical assessment process corresponds to the CROSS evidence strength taxonomy at the independent attestation tier. In the CROSS taxonomy, the highest evidence strength tier requires that claims be assessed by a party independent from the program, using documented methodology, with the assessment record retained in the Attestation Corpus. The REDD+ two-step is the sovereign-scale implementation of this pathway: the Results Report (submission) corresponds to CROSS's completion gate documentation, and the UNFCCC technical review (separate assessment) corresponds to the independent attestation event. The separation between submission and assessment is not merely procedural; it is the mechanism by which independent attestation is structurally enforced.

REDD+ results-based payments, under which funding is disbursed only after verified results have been demonstrated, correspond to the CROSS retroactive obligation mode combined with the completion gate sequence. In retroactive obligation mode, CROSS programs commit to outcomes before implementation, collect evidence during implementation, and trigger disbursement only after the completion gate evidence has been reviewed and found adequate. This is structurally identical to REDD+ results-based payments: achievement must be demonstrated before resources are released. The completion gate is the mechanism by which retroactive payment conditions are enforced in a structured, auditable way.

The national forest monitoring system precondition maps to the CROSS entry gate requirement that data collection capacity be established before the round opens. Under CROSS, programs cannot open an application round if the data collection systems required to measure specified outcomes do not yet exist. A CROSS entry gate for a REDD+-linked program would require documentation that the national or subnational monitoring system is operational and capable of producing the data required for the outcome indicators specified at that gate.

| Warsaw Framework Requirement | CROSS Mechanism |
|---|---|
| FREL/FRL mandatory counterfactual baseline | CROSS counterfactual baseline requirement at entry gate |
| Two-step technical assessment (submission + separate review) | CROSS independent attestation pathway (completion gate + independent attestation event) |
| Results-based payments (disbursement after verified results) | CROSS retroactive obligation mode + completion gate sequence |
| National forest monitoring system as precondition | CROSS entry gate data collection capacity requirement |
| UNFCCC biennial transparency report submission pathway | CROSS Attestation Corpus (persistent record available for inclusion in transparency reporting) |

## How WALKRI Complements This Alignment

WALKRI ensures that intake instruments for REDD+-linked programs specify forest type, monitoring methodology, reference period, and evidence access path before any project registers. In a REDD+ context, these are not optional metadata fields; they are the parameters that determine whether a project's monitoring data can be aggregated with or compared against the national FREL/FRL. A project monitoring tropical peat swamp forest using one set of emission factors cannot be meaningfully compared with a project monitoring dryland tropical forest using different factors unless both projects' intake instruments have specified their monitoring methodology in operationally defined terms.

WALKRI's requirement that evidence access paths be specified at the intake stage also addresses a common failure mode in REDD+ project registration: projects are registered with references to future data that does not yet exist in accessible form, and verification bodies subsequently find that the evidence required for the technical review cannot be produced in the format required. A WALKRI-compliant intake instrument for a REDD+-linked program would specify, for each outcome indicator, which monitoring system will produce the data, in what format, at what frequency, with what chain of custody, making the evidence access path verifiable before any implementation commitment is made.
